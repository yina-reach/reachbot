Timing & Philosophy of Planning
- Planning is crucial for execution but timing matters: start only after proving market demand and product efficacy (someone will buy your stuff, and the stuff works).
- Adopt a continuous, lightweight planning cycle (plan, test, get results, plan again) akin to agile processes; this speeds up reaching product-market fit (PMF).
- Avoid "giant, beefy financial models"; instead, use "super lightweight tools that don't require a forensic accountant" for continuous planning.
- Effective planning aligns all stakeholders on "a set of factors that have to align to move the business forward."
Core Planning Framework
- Identify your startup's primary overarching goal: reaching profitability, raising the next round of capital, or exiting the business.
- Chart a path forward in three steps: 1) predict your Key Performance Indicators (KPIs), 2) set market-driven targets, and 3) determine the activities to achieve those targets.
- Good planning works both bottoms-up (e.g., "how many new customers can the average salesperson close?") and tops-down (e.g., "we need $3M revenue to raise the next round").
- Always start with bottoms-up assumptions; this makes the plan actionable and intellectually honest, unlike purely tops-down target setting.
Granular Revenue & Expense KPIs
- Revenue Assumptions: Focus on granular funnel metrics like Marketing Qualified Leads (MQLs), Sales Qualified Leads (SQLs), pilot conversion rates, churn, Average Contract Value (ACV), and sales cycle length.
- Variable Expense Assumptions: Detail Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), revenue quota per Account Executive (AE), MQLs per Sales Development Representative (SDR), customer support capacity per team/customer, and hosting costs.
- Non-Variable Expense Assumptions: Project product and engineering staffing needs, as well as general & administrative (SG&A) costs, typically for the next 12 months.
- Tracking these specific assumptions allows you to forensically pinpoint *why* performance is off plan (e.g., "I thought three out of four pilots would convert, but only one out of ten did").
- Boards "respect founders who know this level of information" and can translate misses into actionable insights.
Marrying KPIs with Market Targets
- After establishing your bottoms-up KPIs, identify the market-driven targets required to achieve your primary goal (e.g., for a fundraise, investors may expect 100% YoY ARR growth, >70% gross margins, and <1-year CAC payback).
- Compare your plausible bottoms-up KPIs against these external market demands to reveal any "gap" between what you think is possible and what's required.
- If a significant gap exists, consider three choices: 1) modify your underlying assumptions, 2) adjust your capital/expense plan to extend runway, or 3) reconsider your primary goal.
- Use "dead simple spreadsheets" to integrate these two perspectives and visualize expected ARR growth, customer counts, and financial health.
Operationalizing Plans with Goals
- Implement a goal framework (e.g., OKRs, V2MOMs, EOS); the specific framework is less important than having a "simple, inspirational way to understand the priorities."
- Objectives are short, inspirational phrases (e.g., "Create a more efficient marketing engine").
- Key Results (KRs) are measurable, specific, and time-bound (SMART) metrics that define success (e.g., "Lower CAC to $5,000," "Increase ACV to $15,000," "Increase pilot conversion to 75%").
- Actions are the concrete experiments or initiatives undertaken to achieve the Key Results (e.g., "Launch a customer referral program," "Build and launch Fur Bit Pro by September").
- Goals align teams on what matters most, clarify what to work on (and "what we're not going to work on"), promote memory, and establish accountability.
Sustaining Planning & Culture
- Annually, the leadership team (3-7 people) should define company-wide goals, KPIs, and market targets, then review and refine them quarterly.
- Delegate goal-setting: empower individual functional leaders (e.g., Head of Marketing, Sales) to create their own group-level goals that feed into company-wide objectives.
- Cultivate a culture of regular review (weekly or monthly) and accountability; leaders must set the expectation that "this is important. We're going to be reviewing this."
- While the CEO often leads the initial setup, a Head of Operations or Chief of Staff can support the tactical execution and ensure timely input from teams.
Integrated Clarity & Control
- The harmony of revenue/cost assumptions (KPIs), strategic milestones (fundraise/profit/exit), and market targets provides a comprehensive view of business performance.
- When actual performance deviates from projections, the detailed KPIs and assumptions allow for a "forensic" drill-down to identify precisely "where you went wrong."
- This systematic approach brings "some sense of control and calm" to the inherent chaos of startups, enabling faster understanding of misses and quicker corrective action.
- Keep the core 10 revenue and 10 expense KPIs simple and transparent; this reduces intimidation, fosters clarity, and aligns the entire company towards shared goals.
