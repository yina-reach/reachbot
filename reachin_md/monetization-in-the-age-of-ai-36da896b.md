Pricing Framework - 7 Key Levers
1. Metric: what you charge per/for (users, usage, tokens, outcomes)
  - Most important decision, hardest to change, biggest revenue impact
1. Structure: how metric relates to time/volume (tiers, overages, minimums)
1. Level: actual price amount ($10 vs $11) - least interesting, short-term impact only
1. Packaging: how value organized into discrete components
1. Bundling: what features go in which tiers
1. Promotions: freemium, free trial, discounts
1. Monetization strategy: deliberate over/under charging for strategic reasons
AI Pricing Mistake #1: Obsessing Over Costs
- Common concern: AI has marginal costs, what to do?
- Answer: probably nothing unless <50% gross margins
- Cost analysis examples:
  - Fixed costs: don’t change optimal pricing at all
  - Variable costs: change optimal price slightly, but minimal impact above 50% margins
- Recommendation: price to maximize willingness to pay, handle costs separately
AI Pricing Mistake #2: Wrong Price Metrics
- Popular belief: “seats are dying, everything needs credits/tokens”
- Reality: good pricing practice applies regardless of AI
- Four criteria for good metrics:
  - Feasible: can track and enforce
  - Communicable: customers understand and accept it (do they understand tokens?)
  - Segmentable: wide dispersion between smallest/largest customers
  - Valuable: predicts willingness to pay
- Key insight: market matters more than product for metric selection
AI Pricing Mistake #3: Packaging Complexity
- Packaging spectrum: buffet (everyone gets everything) → dim sum (everything custom)
- Rule: start simple, move complex as market matures
- Good-better-best works when:
  - Bigger customers need more features
  - Features nest (gold includes silver includes bronze)
- Use case packaging when different customers need different feature combinations
- Most AI products currently show power law, not bell curve distribution
AI Pricing Mistake #4: Problematic Models
- Outcomes-based pricing challenges:
  - True outcomes pricing requires agreeing on attribution percentage
  - Customers resist paying percentage of business results
  - Recommendation: message outcomes, don’t price on them
- Token-based pricing problems:
  - Cursor example: changed token calculations, customer backlash
  - Customers can’t predict token needs
  - Communicability issues
  - Cost-focused messaging alienates customers
Willingness to Pay Research Methods
- Van Westendorp method (works with 2-2000 customers):
  - “At what price is this a bargain?”
  - “At what price is it expensive but you’d consider it?” ← This is willingness to pay
  - Optional: “Too expensive to buy?” and “So cheap you’d worry about quality?”
- Gabor-Granger method (survey-based, needs 100+ responses):
  - Progressive price testing until customer changes mind
  - Creates discrete data points for correlation analysis
Trial Strategy Framework
- Four models based on value delivery:
  - Freemium: value grows gradually over time (Dropbox)
  - Free trial: value pops immediately and stays constant (Netflix)
  - Deep discount: integration costs/delays (Verizon)
  - Reverse trial: accelerate time to value with more features (Comcast)
