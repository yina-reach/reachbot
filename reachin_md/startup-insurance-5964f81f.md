Why Early Insurance Matters
- 0% of first-time founders buy insurance at formation, compared to 65% of experienced founders who understand the value.
- Startups face increased litigation due to market volatility, leading to spikes in D&O, EPL, and cybercrime lawsuits.
- Key milestones requiring insurance include hiring the first W2 employee, onboarding customers, raising institutional capital, and securing office space.
- Basic insurance can start at $200 at formation; costs will scale up as your company grows and raises funds.
- Specific industries like Fintech, HealthTech, EdTech, and Marketplaces carry unique, higher-risk exposures that require tailored coverage.
Safeguarding Your Workforce
- **Employment Practices Liability (EPL)** is crucial: 40% of private companies are sued by an employee (e.g., wrongful termination, harassment, discrimination).
- EPL claims often range from $75,000 to $150,000, primarily due to defense costs; early settlement can mitigate these expenses.
- **Workers' Compensation** is federally and state-mandated for your first W2 employee; integrate it with your payroll provider to avoid state fines.
- Proactive HR practices are key: implement employee handbooks (signed), clear hiring/firing protocols, regular feedback/documentation, and mandatory HR training.
Protecting Business Assets
- **General Liability (GL)** is typically required by landlords and covers common incidents like slip-and-falls in the office or damage caused in another's space.
- GL policies include "advertising injury" coverage, protecting against false advertising, copyright, trademark, and patent infringement lawsuits.
- **Business Personal Property (BPP)** covers company assets (e.g., laptops, office equipment) from physical damage (fire, water) or theft.
- BPP extends to remote employees' company-owned equipment (e.g., a spilled coffee on a $5,000 laptop, exceeding a typical $500 deductible).
- Claims for BPP are typically settled within 24-48 hours with a receipt of purchase; theft claims require a police report.
Navigating Digital & Contract Risks
- Before engaging customers, ensure your **Terms of Service (ToS)** are buttoned down and contracts include "hold harmless" and "indemnification" language; seek legal review.
- When entering B2B contracts, particularly with large enterprises (schools, universities), negotiate insurance requirements (e.g., $5M-$10M limits are common but costly).
- Start with a foundational **$1M Cyber and Errors & Omissions (E&O)** policy to negotiate from, and re-evaluate as contract values increase.
- **Errors & Omissions (E&O)** covers financial losses from software failure, performance issues, or errors in service (e.g., miscalculating loan payments).
- **Cyber Insurance** is comprehensive, covering data breaches, ransomware payments, phishing attacks (including wire fraud), and first/third-party breaches (PII compromise).
- Robust cyber risk management includes dual-factor authorization, two-person approval for large financial transfers, and continuous team education on phishing.
Executive & Investor Protections
- **Directors & Officers (D&O)** insurance protects founders, executives, and investors from lawsuits related to management decisions.
- D&O is especially critical for regulated industries (EdTech, HealthTech, Fintech), where 40% of claims arise from non-compliance with regulatory guidelines (e.g., Lambda School's filing issue).
- It also covers mismanagement of company funds, misprojected forecasts, and is often a requirement for M&A due diligence.
- Secure D&O when forming a board or raising institutional capital (e.g., Series A), as it protects board members, advisors, and board observers.
- Consider a minimum of $1M in D&O coverage early, especially if operating in a highly regulated industry.
Additional Coverages & Best Practices
- **Crime Insurance** protects against first-party theft, such as an employee stealing company assets or fraudulently wiring funds to themselves.
- While not critical at the earliest stage, implement dual-factor authorization and review all large money transfers to prevent internal fraud.
- **Fiduciary Liability** (for 401k/benefits) and **Media Liability** (for proprietary content against plagiarism/copyright infringement) are usually needed at later stages or for specific content-heavy businesses.
- Proactive risk management is as important as insurance: implement comprehensive HR policies, conduct regular cybersecurity training, and enforce multi-factor authentication for all sensitive transactions.
