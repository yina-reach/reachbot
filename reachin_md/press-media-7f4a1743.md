# Media Pitch Process Overview
Earned media can play an important role in helping companies generate excitement, credibility and momentum, and build awareness to attract potential customers, hires and business partners.
We’ve put together this primer to help founders start thinking about engaging with journalists before they need them, how to prepare and craft a story, and what to expect during the process.
## 1. When Should I Start Thinking About Press/PR?
  If you think the press can help accelerate your growth, think about leveraging them as soon as you can.
  Many startups wait too long before building a relationship with journalists, and only reach out when they have a story or announcement to pitch. This is often unsuccessful for a couple of reasons: In today’s environment, journalists are being pitched around the clock, and their ability to cover a story is often booked in advance. Also, reaching out only when you have something to pitch can make the exchange feel transactional, leading journalists to feel they are viewed simply as a megaphone for your story.
  👉  Approach relationship building with journalists as you would any potential business partner or investor: Start before you have a hard ask or need.
### Identifying Outlets and Reporters
  Whatever industry or market you’re in, you are already reading media coverage of that space. Ask yourself:
  - Which media outlets stand out?
  - Which ones speak to specific audiences you want to get in front of?
  - Who has covered funding announcements in your space?
  - Which stories have featured your potential or current customers and competitors?
  After you’ve identified target outlets, go a step further and identify reporters who cover  your specific topic. A business/tech publication like TechCrunch has an education reporter. An education outlet like Education Week will have dedicated reporters focused on policy, teaching, finance, etc. Most reporters will have a profile page of all the stories they’ve published. Doing your homework on who covers what and being targeted in your outreach will maximize your chances of getting interest.
  - Don’t discount local outlets! Yes — the NYTimes, Bloomberg, TechCrunch and national media will get the most eyeballs. But don’t overlook the regional outlets, like the Business Journals, that cover highlights in their communities, whether it’s an exciting business (yours) or something cool happening in schools. Depending on your story and your goals, consider regional outlets.

### Tips for Reaching Out (Ahead of Time)
  Don’t wait until you have a pitch before reaching out to reporters. If you are already following their work, let them know! Reporters don’t respond to every email but still they like to hear from readers. Here are a few ideas for reaching out:
  - Did you like a particular story? Simply let them know!
  - Do you have unique data or fresh perspectives pertinent to a story?
  - Can you share something to help a reporter better understand the sector?
  👉  In general, offering to be a helpful resource is a great way to build trust and credibility. Done well, you and the reporter may set up regular check-in calls to share your pulse and perspective on what’s happening!
## 2. Preparing Your Story
  There is a lot of business news these days (especially as they relate to funding). Journalists generally do not respond on-demand (unless it is a groundbreaking news involving high-profile names), and most appreciate (and need) some lead time to prepare to write a story. The timeline from pitching your story to seeing it published can take anywhere from a week to over a month.
### What Are Your Goals?
  You should have a set of goals for any story in the press. Below are some questions to help you think through what success would look like.
  - What audience are you trying to reach? (i.e. recruiting new talent; get new users and customers; attract potential business partners and investors; influence decision- and policymakers)
  - What do you want to show to them? (i.e. new funding; influential investors; new product launch; business momentum/milestones; positive impact & outcomes for users; unique insights and perspectives on current trends)
  - Is there a call to action? (i.e. apply to join your team; sign up for a demo; subscribe for updates; try/buy your product; reach out to chat)
### Timing
  While there is no perfect formula to timing, you should consider your news in the broader context of the news cycle. Is there a lot of buzz on topics and markets pertinent to what you’re working on — and thus an opportunity to ride the momentum? (If the market is noisy, how can you distinguish yourself from the noise?) Are there major events (like presidential election season) that will dominate headlines and readers’ attention?
  If your company has a series of exciting developments, you can also consider spacing out announcements over a period of time. Most stories generally cover at least one of the following news items:
  - Funding
  - New executive hire (especially if it is a big name)
  - New product launch
  - Major event
  - Efficacy / impact outcomes
  - Business milestones (signing up a big customer; revenue growth)
  The upside to spacing out stories is that you are extending the period for which you are getting press. The downside is that follow-on stories are not a guarantee; some journalists and outlets prefer to cover several developments in one story, rather than do a series.
### Crafting Your Story
  Making sure that a journalist understands your work is key to ensuring that their readers understand your work. Try to explain things as clearly as possible, and avoid jargon and insider language. Can you describe what your company does in one sentence?
  Here are some common questions you should be prepared to address and answer:
  - Company and Vision: Why did you start the company? Why now? Who’s on your team? (Anyone notable?) What is your mission? Long-term vision? If you are wildly successful, how does the world change?
  - Problem: What problem(s) are you looking to solve? Why is this a meaningful challenge? Why don’t existing services or solutions suffice?
  - Product: What does it do? How does the product work? (Walk me through the user experience.) How do we know it works? Who pays for it?
  - Customer: Who are they? What is their experience with your product? Why do they like it? How have their lives changed? What outcomes do they experience? (Reporters sometimes like to get users’ perspectives, so it’s a good idea to make a couple available for an interview.)
  - Challenges & Priorities: What’s the biggest challenge for you right now? How do you differentiate yourself from peers and competitors? What do you plan to do with the funding? What are your immediate/long-term priorities?
## 3. Pitching Your Story
  Going from pitching a story to seeing it published can take anywhere from a week to a month. Quicker turnarounds do happen but we recommend pitching at least a couple weeks in advance before your desired publish date.
  If you are pitching reporters directly, keep the email pitch concise and succinct. A general sweet spot is a couple paragraphs describing your company, plus three bullet points highlighting metrics and milestones related to the news. You are not trying to tell the entire story in this email; just enough juicy bits to entice them to talk to you to learn more.
  We recommend creating a shortlist of journalists/media outlets you’d like to appear in, and pitch them according to highest to lowest priority. It’s unlikely they will all respond (but if they do, congrats!). If you don’t hear back after a few days, email the others on the list.
  Other materials to consider preparing:
  - Demo account, if available (some journalists like to try out a product to get a better feel and understand for it)
  - Screenshots and images
  - Users, customers, investors and/or other stakeholders available for interview

### Embargoes and Exclusives
  Embargo: An embargo is the time for when the story goes live — when the news is published and announced to the world. It’s up to you to decide if you want an embargo time for your story; common considerations involve timing it with other developments you want to share (like closing a big sales deal), or coordinating the announcement with your customers, partners and/or investors. The media will generally agree to respect your embargo time and not publish the story until the time/date of your choosing.
  ❗If you decide to have an embargo time, make sure you communicate the same embargo time to every publication you pitch. Also make sure that all non-media parties who will share the news also honor the time.
  Exclusives: Top-tier outlets like The New York Times or the Wall Street Journal may only agree to write a story only if you give them an exclusive, which means they get to be the first to publish your news. (The world of media can be hyper competitive when it comes to being the first to break the news.)
  ❗If you want to offer your news as an exclusive, you should only offer an exclusive to one outlet at a time.

## 4. General Interview Protocols
  Once you’ve landed an interview, congrats! There are three standard conditions for interview conversations:
  - On the record: When you are doing an interview with a reporter who is responding to a pitch, s/he will generally consider that conversation, by default, to be on the record. That means that anything you say can be published and attributed/quoted to you. Some reporters will be explicit in letting you know beforehand that a conversation is on the record; either way it is a good practice to confirm.
  - On background: If there is something you want to share, but do not want to be quoted or attributed to you, you can ask for that portion to be “on background.” Reporters generally prefer not talking on background, but may agree if the information you have is valuable or otherwise hard to get.
  - Off the record: You can also ask for parts to be off the record, which means whatever information you share cannot be published. Reporters generally avoid this but will agree when their desire to know is greater than their desire to publish.
  ❗When in doubt, ask and clarify which condition you are speaking under. Jumping between different conditions in the same conversation is not unusual, but it can sometimes be easy to mix up which parts are on record or not. It’s generally simplest and safest to assume that all interview conversations are on the record.
  ⚠️ Remember: You can always decline to comment on questions that you don’t feel comfortable responding to.
  ⚠️  Reporters generally will not let you review the story before publication. But you can and should offer to help fact-check any details you shared.
