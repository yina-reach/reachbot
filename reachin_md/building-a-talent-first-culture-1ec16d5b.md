# Building a Talent-First Culture

Reach Capital Session Recordings

**Date:** 2022-04-01
**Tags:** hiring, people ops
**Recording:** [Recording](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1QJ5fOVdy8VaVu_N8QqJY0LD9-JAO-jhY/view)
**Speaker:** [Andrea Robb](https://www.linkedin.com/in/andrearobb/) (Vibrancy, Airbnb, Autodesk)

Culture Scales with Growth
- Becky Karsh's experience spans 1-person startups (Beyond Business) to 30,000-person tech giants (Uber, Snap), illustrating how culture shifts at every stage.
- At 1-10 people, focus on getting the work out; explicit values are "back of mind" as implicit founder ethos dominates.
- At 50-70 people, founder's ethos deeply permeates; this is the stage to start thinking with intentionality about how you show up.
- Uber demonstrated how both positive and negative founder traits (e.g., "move fast, steamroll") scaled dramatically, leading to crises like lacking an HR/employee relations group at 5,000 people.
- Fenix (100 people) excelled due to founder Richie's "super intentional" culture, inspired by Bill Walsh's "The Score Takes Care of Itself."
- Snapchat's current culture, "kind, smart, creative," is a founder-led (Evan & Bobby) and constantly reinforced, permeating globally as part of the company's DNA.
Prioritizing People Systems Early
- The full employee lifecycle covers attraction, hiring, onboarding, development/growth, engagement/retention, and exiting/alumni relations.
- Initially, focus intensely on Hiring (your first people hire is often a recruiter) and Onboarding (setting new hires up for success).
- Quickly integrate Development & Growth practices to manage performance and facilitate career movement (e.g., new experiences every 1.5-2 years).
- Engagement and retention programs can come later; at early stages, the excitement of "zero to one" building often acts as a self-sustaining motivator.
Cultivating a Feedback-First Environment
- Build habits that scale: A culture lacking fast feedback will scale, leading to decreased productivity as people become shy about giving honest input.
- Frame feedback as neutral and for growth: It's about improving work and the business, not personal attacks; for example, address specific actions like "redlining every document" instead of vague characterizations.
- Establish trust proactively: Especially in remote settings, build a foundation of trust where everyone is committed to making each other better.
- Leaders must model the behavior: Actively ask for and visibly accept feedback to demonstrate its value and importance.
- Balance positive and redirecting feedback: A consistent mix prevents a "dark" feedback culture where only critical input is given.
- Encourage active reception: Advise individuals to "try on" feedback, assuming it's correct for a moment to foster self-awareness.
Driving Performance & Accountability
- Implement early, lightweight performance check-ins: Avoid large, cumbersome processes initially. Focus on quarterly check-ins or annual reviews that serve the work.
- Set clear expectations: Utilize OKRs or other goal-setting frameworks to provide critical clarity for individual and team performance.
- Consider performance ratings and linked compensation: Snap uses quarterly ratings with "top/exceeds" bonuses, designed with the CEO, to reward in-moment behavior, which has shown to increase retention and motivation.
- Address low performance decisively: Low performers (e.g., 50% output) cause downstream issues like resentment, increased workload for others, and a lowered bar for "A players."
- Act on low performance: Exiting underperformers, while difficult, is often appreciated by teams and can "unlock" faster overall progress ("going slow to go fast").
- Differentiate between potential and poor fit: Assess if an individual needs coaching (competence or cultural alignment) or if they are simply in the wrong role or situation.
Designing Your People Systems
- Appoint a dedicated owner: Early HR hires are often "jack-of-all-trades" who can own these systems, manage HRIS, and ensure compliance.
- Choose appropriate tools: Start simple with Google Docs or spreadsheets for documentation; more robust HRIS platforms offer performance modules. Ensure thoughtful documentation for future reference.
- Define clear cultural values: Founders should lead this process, focusing on desired behaviors and outcomes rather than just "cute" semantics. Values should be slightly aspirational but feel authentic.
- Integrate values into performance: Decide if and how values will be measured in performance reviews. Established models like Korn Ferry behavioral competencies can provide structure.
- Avoid overly rigorous competency models: These can unintentionally become simple checklists for promotions, fostering a toxic "checkbox culture."
The Founder's Unrivaled Role
- Culture comes from the top: Everyone in the organization watches the founders and leaders; "practice what you preach" is paramount.
- Be the exemplar: Founders must act as "the parent in the room," owning company messages and demonstrating desired behaviors, even in challenging situations (e.g., layoffs).
- Cultivate a "leveled-up" mindset: Make difficult decisions for the greater good of the company and its investors, looking beyond individual preferences.
- Create extreme clarity: Providing clear goals and expectations is the "biggest superpower" of any organization, empowering rapid movement.
- Multiply impact through others: Actively delegate tasks and responsibilities to avoid owning everything, which empowers your team and scales your influence.
- Develop a leadership narrative: Articulate what you care about, your commitments to the team, and your expectations to provide consistent guidance.
Be Intentional About Culture
- Unintentional culture has downstream effects: What "just happened" at 10 people can become a deeply ingrained and problematic norm at 100, 500, or 1,000 employees.
- Proactive design prevents future crises: Thinking through people systems early on avoids the need to fix major, complex issues as the company grows.
- Consistency beats perfection: Implement systems that your company can realistically maintain, even if lightweight (e.g., annual reviews vs. quarterly); "beat the drum" consistently about their importance.
