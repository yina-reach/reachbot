On Explaining Data to the Public
- Core philosophy: People can understand graphs and data when presented clearly
  - “I have built a career on the idea that all people can understand graphs”
  - First book (Expecting Better) included graphs for lay audiences - unusual for parenting books
- Important to explain why, not just what
  - When people understand the reasoning behind recommendations, they’re more likely to trust and use the information
  - Example: Other pregnancy books might say “it’s fine to have a glass of wine” without explaining why
  - Providing explanation helps people navigate conflicting advice
- Powerful tool: Helping people feel smart
  - “If you can make people feel smart, then you will have them forever”
Danger of Correlation vs Causation Case Study: Breastfeeding and IQ Data
- Common claim: Breastfeeding leads to higher IQ in children
- Initial studies:
  - Show correlation between breastfeeding and cognitive development
  - Basic adjustments for some demographic factors still show positive effects
  - Most literature stops here with incomplete analysis
- Better analysis:
  - When adjusting for mother’s IQ, effect size shrinks dramatically
  - When comparing siblings (same mother, different feeding methods), effect disappears completely
- Takeaway: Show how progressively better methodologies change results
  - Takes representative paper and walks through it in detail rather than overwhelming with many studies
Audience Considerations
- Different audiences (university students, parents, educators) have more similarities than differences. Key differences are in institutional knowledge, not data comprehension
  - “You don’t need to be as different as you think.”
- Everyone struggles with data literacy questions, regardless of education/background. But if something truly makes sense, it should be explainable to everyone
Platform Considerations
- Instagram (500,000 followers) different from books or academic writing
- Building trust on social media requires personal connection
  - “To get people on Instagram or social media to listen to you about data, you also have to be their friend”
  - Intersperse data explanations with personal content
  - “I love running. You know, here’s me eating…”
- Creating trust in a person is easier than creating trust in a brand
Communication Best Practices
- Avoid jargon: Biggest challenge when transitioning from academic writing to public communication
- For research with incomplete long-term data: Be honest about limitations - “this is what we know now, this is when we will learn more”
  - Don’t overclaim - overclaiming loses trust
- When sharing product data:
  - Explain methodology in detail rather than just claiming “our product is better”
  - “The more you can say about what we did, here is what the data looks like”
- Balance information without overwhelming:
  - Read hundreds of papers but highlight only representative examples
  - “Show me your best two graphs” - constrain researchers who want to show too much
- Data literacy is poor but teachable
  - Core statistics concepts should be taught in schools
  - Focus on conceptual understanding (random sampling, etc.) rather than formulas
Q&A Highlights
- Handling criticism:
  - Having confidence in your analysis helps withstand criticism
  - Support system that isn’t involved in social media provides perspective
- Education research challenges:
  - Implementation gap between controlled studies and real-world application
  - Example: High-dose tutoring works in studies but gets watered down in practice
  - Need more researchers evaluating programs at scale, not just small controlled studies
- Quick test for evaluating research:
  - Ask: “Is it likely this behavior is randomly chosen?”
  - If not (e.g., screen time studies comparing 4 hours vs. 0), causation claims are suspect
