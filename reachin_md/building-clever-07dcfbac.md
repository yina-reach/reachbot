What is Business Development?
- **Strategic Partnerships**: BD creates binding legal relationships between two companies to achieve mutual goals.
- **Purpose**: Aims to create inflection points for growth, revenue, market expansion, or access to new technology/features.
- **Key Deal Types**:
- **Distribution**: Most common for startups. Leverage a partner's customer base (e.g., placing your product in their store, using their sales team as a channel).
- **Revenue-Driving**: Sell your user base or traffic to a partner (e.g., Facebook powering search via Microsoft pre-ads). Less common for early startups.
- **Licensing**: Inbound (license technology/data into your company) or Outbound (license your IP/tech out, e.g., Marvel IP for games).
- **BD vs. Sales**: BD involves complex, high-stakes, potentially game-changing but expensive, one-off contracts. Sales focuses on scaling repeatable revenue channels and lowering customer acquisition costs.
- **BD as a Catalyst**: Use initial BD deals to prototype and template a repeatable process that can then be scaled through a sales organization.
Strategic BD Engagement for Startups
- **Default Caution**: Startups generally should avoid early BD due to minimal leverage and high distraction risk. BD can consume significant time without tangible results.
- **Founder-Led**: If pursuing BD, the CEO or founder should lead to ensure strategic alignment and a high bar for time investment.
- **Measure Progress with Term Sheets**: True progress is indicated when both parties actively engage in drafting and negotiating a term sheet, not just initial conversations.
- **Know Your Alternatives (BATNA)**: Understand your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement and your partner's. Be prepared to walk away, as deals often die at the last minute.
- **Maintain Momentum**: Close easy points early, always schedule the next meeting before the current one ends, and proactively follow up to counter inertia from larger partners.
Negotiation Principles & Preparation
- **Use a Term Sheet**: Negotiate off a comprehensive term sheet, not emails or the long-form contract. Negotiate the entire sheet at once to enable trade-offs across terms.
- **In-Person Advantage**: While challenging now, in-person negotiation builds trust and accelerates progress on difficult issues better than remote interactions.
- **The "Middle Ground"**: Be aware that many contentious points in negotiation often resolve in the middle of both parties' initial offers.
- **Anchoring**: Strategically set your initial offer or ask (e.g., a number, an aggressive term) to "anchor" the negotiation, pulling the eventual middle ground closer to your desired outcome.
- **Stocking Horse**: Cultivate a credible competitive threat (e.g., another potential partner like Salesforce) to improve your leverage, even if subtly mentioned.
Communication & Psychology in Negotiation
- **Understand Counterpart Dynamics**: Know who the ultimate decision-maker is (often not your direct contact) and what motivates them (e.g., how the deal helps their career). Junior BD teams may say "no" repeatedly until their boss is engaged.
- **Don't Talk Too Much**: Avoid negotiating against yourself by filling silence. State your ask, then stop talking, allowing the other party to respond.
- **Strategic "Yes"**: Don't agree immediately, even to seemingly obvious points. Save your "yesses" for leverage, and be precise; "okay" or "I understand" is not a commitment.
- **Direct & Strategic Communication**: Always tell the truth to avoid future pitfalls. Be specific about your wants. Make even small "gives" seem like a big deal to imply value and extract concessions.
- **Maintain Professionalism**: Don't take disagreements personally; it's business. Use a smile to diffuse tension after a challenging ask, making your stance appear less confrontational.
Legal & Contractual Terms
- **Strategic Lawyer Engagement**: You'll need legal counsel, but do much of the initial editing on terms yourself to save on legal fees before sending to your lawyer. Be aware lawyers prefer to speak with other lawyers.
- **"Having the Pen"**: Control the drafting of the contract/term sheet. This allows you to frame language in your favor and speed up the process.
- **Exclusivity**: A common and difficult ask, especially for startups. Seek to limit exclusivity by time, geography, or scope (e.g., a temporary "launch partner exclusivity").
- **"Scary" Terms**: Indemnification and Limitation of Liability are serious but rarely invoked unless things go very wrong. For a startup, the difference between a high or low liability cap might not matter if the company is already failing.
- **Commitment Levels**: Understand terms like "will," "best efforts," "commercially reasonable efforts," and "reasonable efforts," as these dictate the enforceability and strength of commitments.
- **"Spirit of the Deal"**: Non-binding, soft language about mutual goals can be valuable for aligning partners and reminding them of the relationship's original intent.
Post-Closing & Partner Management
- **Clear the Air**: After signing, address any lingering hard feelings. Send congratulations, give significant credit to your counterparts, and celebrate the successful closing.
- **Transition to Management**: Business development professionals are often not ideal long-term partner managers. Hand off the relationship to a dedicated account or partner management team for ongoing execution.
- **Formalize Engagement**: Establish regular check-ins at both working and senior levels to maintain momentum, ensure performance, and continue building high-level relationships.
- **Leverage PR**: Utilize strategic partnerships as opportunities for positive press and communications to amplify your company's story and growth.
