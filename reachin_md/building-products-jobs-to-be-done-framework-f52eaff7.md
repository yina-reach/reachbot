Why Use Jobs to Be Done?
- The Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) framework helps founders build products that solve *real* problems, not just perceived ones, by understanding customer behavior and their underlying motivations.
- JTBD moves beyond asking customers what they want (e.g., "faster horses") to identifying their unmet aspirations and struggles (e.g., "better transportation"). People don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole.
- Signs you need JTBD: over-reliance on your own vision (confirmation bias), translating research directly into products without accounting for user motivation, prioritizing technical challenges over user needs, or team misalignment on your product's core value proposition.
- A lack of shared empathy for user problems across product, marketing, and engineering can lead to diffuse strategy and execution challenges.
Understanding Your Users' "Job"
- Shift focus from merely building a product to enabling users with "superpowers" to overcome barriers and achieve their desired outcomes.
- Deeply understand "people problems" by exploring underlying motivations, pain points, and existing barriers that prevent users from achieving what they want.
- Conduct user interviews to uncover three key areas: 1) Their underlying motivation and context for a task, 2) The specific barriers preventing them, and 3) What they are currently "hiring" (using/hacking together) or "firing" (not using) to get the job done.
- Map market opportunity by assessing the importance of a job to users (market demand) against how well existing solutions satisfy it (market gap). Focus on areas with high importance and low satisfaction.
Crafting Your JTBD Statement
- A clear JTBD statement articulates precisely what a specific group of people wants to accomplish in a specific circumstance, and their barriers to getting it.
- Use a simple, effective structure: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], but [barrier], help me [solution] so that I can [desired outcome]."
- While initial research may reveal many potential jobs, narrow your focus to 1-2 core jobs per key stakeholder to ensure clarity and a sharp value proposition for your product.
JTBD in Practice: LumiKids & Tuned
- **LumiKids (Lumosity Kids):** Successfully balanced two stakeholders – kids (users) and parents (buyers). Kids' job: engaging, magical experiences with a sense of agency. Parents' job: "feeling like a good parent" (e.g., fostering happiness or learning).
- Parent communication and branding were heavily informed by this insight, often delivered *outside* the app (e.g., email marketing) to avoid interfering with the child's independent play experience.
- **Tuned (Facebook NPE):** Identified a significant market gap for "emotional connection" in close relationships through extensive surveys. This led to focusing on millennial couples.
- The Tuned app's JTBD: "When I want to feel connected to my partner, but I need some creative inspiration, help me be more emotionally expressive so that I can strengthen my relationship."
- This iterative process, grounded in the JTBD framework, led to a 3X increase in retention for Tuned, demonstrating its effectiveness in achieving product-market fit.
Building Products with JTBD
- Begin by developing an "opinionated perspective" on your target user, then thoroughly research existing solutions and competitors to understand the landscape.
- Ground your product principles (3-4 core guiding ideas) in user motivations and desires derived from JTBD statements; use these principles for team alignment and feature prioritization.
- Build a "Minimum Lovable Product" (MLP) – not just the bare minimum to learn, but polished enough to meet modern user expectations and provide a clear, high-quality signal of value.
- Define clear hypotheses and expected metrics/goals for each MLP iteration to quickly validate or invalidate your assumptions.
- Commit to a tight scope and timeline, ruthlessly cutting less critical features (e.g., a quarter of planned projects) to ensure fast, focused learning cycles.
- Release the MLP to gather authentic user reactions, focusing on what they love, what's missing, and what causes pain, then iterate rapidly based on these insights.
Navigating Multiple Stakeholders
- Many products, especially in EdTech, serve different stakeholders (e.g., children as users, parents/administrators as buyers/decision-makers).
- Recognize that each stakeholder has distinct motivations, barriers, and "moments" or triggers for engagement with your product or offering.
- Prioritize stakeholders based on their role in the user journey (e.g., who makes the initial purchase decision versus who is the primary end-user).
- Consider designing separate experiences or interfaces for different stakeholders (e.g., a web portal for administrators, an app for students) to avoid conflicts and optimize for each job to be done.
- Utilize different communication channels to engage each stakeholder, tailoring messaging and product value propositions to their specific jobs (e.g., email marketing for parents/admins, in-app experience for children).
