I am a technology reporter in the business section of The New York Times. I write about how tech companies, digital devices and apps are reshaping childhood, public schooling and job opportunities.
I am fascinated by the far-reaching ways in which social media platforms, artificial intelligence and other technologies are transforming young people’s lives, educational experiences and career prospects. I’m interested in how the business motives of large tech companies and small education start-ups influence public schooling. I’ve learned from experience that the best ideas for stories about tech in schools often come from students and teachers.
My Background
I started at The Times in 2005 as a reporter covering the pharmaceutical industry and cosmetic medical treatments. Since 2013, I’ve written about the intersection of technology, business and education. In 2014, my reporting on student data tracking helped prompt California to enact the Student Online Personal Information Protection Act, a landmark law limiting how school vendors can use students’ personal details. In 2019, I was a member of a Times reporting team whose coverage of tech industry privacy violations won a George Polk Award and was a finalist for a Pulitzer Prize in national reporting.
Before joining The Times, I was a roving correspondent for Outside Magazine, where I covered global warming in the Arctic and the traffic in endangered species in Thailand and Myanmar. Before that, I lived in Russia, where I reported on human rights for the Forward, covered business for USA Today and wrote features for Vogue and GQ. I got my start in journalism as a features intern at The Providence Journal.
I graduated from Brown University with a B.A. in comparative literature and I earned an M.A. in creative writing from Boston University. In the summers, I teach a tech accountability journalism course for high school students attending The School of The New York Times, a pre-college program. I’m currently writing a book for W.W. Norton called “Coding Kids,” on the growth of computer science education in public schools.
Journalistic Ethics
As a Times journalist, I share the values and adhere to the standards of integrity outlined in our Ethical Journalism Handbook. Making sure my stories are accurate and fair is very important to me. In my reporting, I interview multiple experts and participants with different viewpoints. This year, I’m working to include the voices of more young people in my education technology stories — and I take extra care when interviewing students who are under 18. I work to protect my sources. I do not accept gifts, money or favors. I do not participate in politics or make political donations. When I am reporting, I always identify myself as a reporter for The Times.
