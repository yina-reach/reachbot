Fundraising market overview
Q1 2026 was the largest venture capital quarter on record, but 65% of that capital went to four companies. Deal count has dropped below 4,000, down from over 5,000 in Q1 2025. Capital is flowing, but into fewer companies and larger checks.
What this means for founders
- Consider a two-tier fundraising approach. Understand what the mega funds are investing in your sector and whether they're a fit for later rounds.
- There is a real AI premium, but don't force an AI narrative that isn't true. Effective positioning shows how the business fits the shift, or makes a clear case for why it's AI-proof.
- The capital is there. This is a good time to raise, even if the environment requires more strategy than prior years.
- Exit path is increasingly on investors' minds. Don't talk about exits directly, but make a compelling case for market size and sector momentum. Help VCs draw the line from your TAM to the opportunity.
Sector benchmarks
- AI infrastructure, hardware, deep tech, cybersecurity, and semiconductors are commanding premium valuations and outlier round sizes.
- In health, mega deals are skewing averages up. Data moats and consumer health companies are attracting the highest valuations.
- In learning, fewer outlier rounds exist. Native AI learning tools are still raising strong rounds, but diligence now includes more scrutiny around efficacy.
- In work, large rounds are going to companies embedding agentic workflows in specific verticals, with measurable dollar savings, hours reclaimed, or outcomes improved.
- If your company sits at an intersection (edtech + fintech, edtech + hardware), highlight those adjacencies.
Reach portfolio data
- The highest-valued portfolio companies over the last 12 months are those most directly tied to powering the AI economy — compute, data infrastructure, and workflow automation.
- Bridge and extension rounds are now common. Around 16–17% of venture rounds in 2025 were bridge rounds of some kind.
- Bridges aren't inherently negative. In many cases, founders are raising opportunistically to extend runway and reach the next milestone before a larger round. Don't need to overdial on positioning it as a bridge; also frame it as a way for existing investors to increase their positions
- Round size and post-money valuation are loosely correlated, not tightly. There's no rigid formula. That said, there's real concentration around the $50M–$75M post-money range for Series A.
Series A benchmarks in 2026
- Carta data shows top quartile Series A ARR nearing $6.9M, with median at $2.8M.
- Only 10% of companies raise a Series A within 12 months of seed, and 20% within 24 months.
- Quality of revenue matters more than absolute revenue. Growth rate, margins, retention, and efficiency all factor into investor assessment.
- Pitch real ARR only. Pilot ARR, contracted ARR, and run-rate figures presented as equivalent will be seen through. (See slide 23)
- Every raise has exceptions. Some companies raise competitive Series A rounds well under $1M ARR. Others with $3M+ struggle if the story isn't compelling.
How to run the right process
- Start by pulling comps. Look at round size, valuation, lead investor, traction, and the market story comparable companies told.
- Decide early whether you're raising a bridge/extension round or a full Series A.
- For extension rounds, keep the target list tight (10 or fewer) and start with existing investors. Operator funds can be a strong fit here.
- For a full Series A, plan for a broader, more time-intensive process.
More process tips
- Target partners, not firms. One partner may pass while another at the same firm is the right fit. Find the investors publicly passionate about your space.
- Use a few lower-priority meetings early to sharpen the pitch and test objections before your top targets.
- Prepare for off-list reference calls. Pre-brief strong supporters and encourage them to reach out to relevant funds proactively.
- Have your diligence room ready before opening the process.
- Learn to read soft no's. "Keep me posted as the round comes together" is a no.
- Keep building during the raise. New customers, shipped features, and fresh milestones move conversations forward.
- Send weekly updates to your board and active investors so they can back-channel in real time on your behalf.
- Expect a high volume of rejection. Time the raise around the strongest point in your business cycle.
What AI has changed
- AI-native companies carry a 42% valuation premium at seed versus non-AI companies.
- Median AI revenue multiples are running 20–30x, compared to roughly 10x for SaaS.
- Top AI performers are hitting ~$40M ARR in year one. Some companies have reached $100M ARR in under two years.
- The T2D3 benchmark (triple-triple-double-double growth in first few years) no longer reflects the growth pace of top AI-native companies.
What hasn't changed
- Capital efficiency is still the universal language. Burn multiple and CAC payback remain core to how investors evaluate companies.
- Seed dilution holds steady around 20%, regardless of whether a company is AI-native or not.
- Time between rounds has stretched. Plan accordingly.
Founder reflections on fundraising
Jen Wirt, founder and CEO of Coral Care, raised a $13M Series A led by Haymaker Ventures.
- Created a 40-page memo pairing narrative and data, so she could answer any question quickly and consistently. Most investors received a subset, but the exercise forced her to think through every area of the business.
- Raised additional capital before launching the round to enter the market with runway and from a position of strength.
- Ran 83 first meetings intentionally. Volume helped desensitize her to rejections and get interested investors to the same stage simultaneously, creating real momentum.
- AI framing was clear and unapologetic: “The care is AI-proof, but we're powered by AI everywhere around that core human interaction.”
- Sent weekly updates to board and participating investors throughout the process, giving them specific context for back-channeling in real time.
Andrew Carlins, Co-Founder and CEO of Songscription, raised a $5M Seed led by Reach.
- Time kills all deals. Creating honest urgency (closing timelines, real momentum) moves firms to act.
- But don’t manufacture fake urgency. The community is small, word travels fast. If you're closing, say you're closing. If you have a term sheet, say so. Don't claim either if it isn't true.
- The final 72 hours before closing tend to bring the most movement from investors. Block your calendar.
