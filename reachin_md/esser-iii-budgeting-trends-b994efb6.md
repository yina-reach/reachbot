Understanding ESSER III Funding
- ESSER III is a $122 billion federal stimulus passed in March 2021, distributed to K-12 Local Education Authorities (LEAs) based on the Title 1 formula.
- 90% of funds went directly to districts, bypassing state control; states must approve plans but cannot add strings.
- A minimum of 20% of funds must address "learning loss," a hurdle easily met by districts.
- Districts were required to consult the public, pass a plan, and post it on their websites. Burbio has gathered 6,000 such plans, representing 83% of US K-12 students.
Key Spending Areas & Opportunities
- Five main spending buckets: Academic Intervention (largest), Physical & Mental Health, Staffing & Retention, Facilities & Operations, and Technology.
- A typical district plan segments funds into 15-25 specific line items from a total of 100 identified categories.
- Significant categories by dollar amount: Summer/After School/Extended Day ($6.5 billion, second largest), HVAC (largest single category), Tutoring (1,279 districts, $1.2 billion+), Infrastructure & Hardware ($2.1 billion).
- Many funds are in larger, generalized buckets: "Summer/After School" often includes hidden spending on STEM, arts, and curriculum. "Early Childhood" (275 districts spending ~$10M each) can encompass furniture, staff, or facilities.
- Specific technology call-outs include smart panels ($393 million), connectivity ($885 million), and cybersecurity.
ESSER Spending Pace & District Trends
- Initial spending was slow (March 2021-Spring 2022) due to district reopenings, Omicron, and careful planning. "I don't really think they should [spend faster]."
- Spending accelerated in Spring 2022, with 2023 projected as a significant year for fund deployment.
- As of December, over 30% of districts had spent less than 10% of their allocation, while nearly 10% had spent almost all.
- Larger districts, initially slower, are now spending the fastest (e.g., 13% spent in summer to 20% in fall for biggest districts). Smaller districts tended to spend earlier.
- Districts are currently spending, on average, 3-4% of their allocation per month, with approximately 18 months remaining for expenditure.
Flexibility & Changes in ESSER Plans
- Districts can change their ESSER plans, but it's not frequent: less than 10% of districts are altering their plans, and these changes typically involve only about 10% of the money.
- Changes require resubmission to the state for approval.
- Examples of plan changes: funds reallocated from infrastructure projects (e.g., swimming pool construction) to curriculum (e.g., MTSS), or from broad after-school spending to more specific categories like staff and curriculum.
- These changes are generally substantive, reflecting shifts in district priorities or increased specificity, but they are not widespread.
Long-Term Outlook & "Fiscal Cliff" Perspective
- ESSER III represents a substantial short-term increase in K-12 funding: $122 billion over three years, adding roughly $40 billion annually to the $800 billion K-12 budget, a 5% increase.
- Title 1 districts, which serve high-need students, are seeing even larger increases of 15-25% due to the fund allocation formula.
- The "fiscal cliff" narrative is "overwrought terminology." While some districts (those losing enrollment, particularly in large cities or certain "blue" states facing state-level fiscal issues) may face challenges in two years, it won't be universal.
- A McKinsey report (using Burbio data) found 70-80% of districts believe they will "figure it out." States are expected to intervene and ease transitions.
How to Leverage Burbio's Data for Sales & Strategy
- Burbio provides granular data for each district, including NCES ID, enrollment, urban/rural status, total ESSER III allocation, and amount spent, along with a direct link to the full plan document.
- District spending plans are categorized into 100+ sub-categories, indicating dollar amounts or an 'X' for intent to spend, even without a specific amount.
- Data is updated monthly, capturing the latest spending rates from state websites.
- Founders can use this data to:
- Integrate into CRMs (e.g., HubSpot) using NCES IDs for targeted sales outreach.
- Inform email marketing campaigns to districts based on specific spending intentions.
- Educate district contacts: "Our lead contact didn't know the plan had their money in it." Decision-makers often aren't aware of specific line items in their own ESSER plans, creating an opportunity for vendors to highlight relevant allocations.
- Burbio is introducing keyword searching to identify mentions of specific vendor names (e.g., "Chromebooks," "iPads") or curriculum within plan documents.
- The data is designed for high ROI, enabling one or two closed deals to cover the cost.
