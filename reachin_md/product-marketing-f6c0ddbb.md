What is a Brand?
- A brand is not what *you* think it is, but what *other people think you are*.
- The goal of positioning and messaging is to guide these perceptions.
- Volvo's brand association with safety comes from 60+ years of consistent product and marketing decisions, not just its logo.
- Early-stage founders often over-invest in logo design instead of building brand through action.
Core Pillars of Branding
- Purpose: The change you want to see in the world.
- Position: What makes your product unique and different relative to what your audience already knows.
- Personality: How your brand shows up in the world (copy, visuals, tone).
- Focus on articulating positioning clearly, as purpose is often already clear to founders.
The Positioning Framework
- Target Customer: Define precisely who you are serving.
- Problem/Need: Identify the specific pain point or opportunity.
- Category: Determine the market space you operate in (existing, modified, or new).
- Benefit: The desired outcome for the customer.
- Foil (Competing Alternative): The alternative a customer might consider instead of your product (not always a direct competitor).
- Point of Differentiation: What makes your benefit unique and sets you apart from the foil.
Category Strategy Decisions
- Existing Category: Playing in a known market with established players (e.g., Gmail in free webmail).
- Modified Category: Taking an existing category and adding a new dimension (e.g., Nest adding "learning" to thermostats).
- New Category: Creating an entirely new market space (e.g., Eero as a "Home Wi-Fi System").
- Creating a new category is harder but can lead to market dominance; modifying an existing one can capture existing budgets more easily.
Positioning Workshop Example: Yay Lunch
- Target Audience: Busy professional parents with school-aged children (ages 5-14) who value convenience.
- Problem: Lack of time and dissatisfaction with current options (brown-bagging, school-provided lunches) leading to parental guilt or concern about child's eating habits.
- Category: Considered "daily lunch service," but "school lunch delivery" or "kids' lunch service" might be more precise to communicate the offering to parents.
- Key Benefit: Convenience for parents and peace of mind knowing their child is eating a good meal.
- Foil: The hassle of brown-bagging.
Hiring Your First Marketer
- Generally, a dedicated marketer is needed when a company reaches ~10 employees.
- The specific role (product marketer, acquisition, growth) depends on product team capabilities and user acquisition channels.
- For B2B products with decision-makers who don't use the product, messaging must resonate with both the buyer and the end-user.
- Product marketing teams are crucial when product management is less focused on customer needs and messaging.
