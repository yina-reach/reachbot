Early-Stage Revenue Forecasting
- Focus on individual accounts in your CRM (e.g., HubSpot) and apply founder's gut feel on close probability and timing.
- Best for early-stage companies with a handful of customers or when dealing with super outlier deals (e.g., $5M+ deals at large companies like Renaissance).
- Effective for short-term forecasting (3-6 months), as pipeline visibility is limited further out.
- Relies on founder honesty and lack of commission bias for realistic assessments.
Pipeline-Based Forecasting
- Categorize deals by CRM stages (e.g., Discovery, Negotiation, Chase PO/Commit, Closed Won) to standardize the process.
- Calculate historical conversion rates for each stage (e.g., Chase PO to Close Won ~88%, Negotiation ~50%).
- Forecast revenue by multiplying current pipeline deals by their stage-specific close probability, typically on a quarterly basis.
- Requires consistent sales training on stage definitions to ensure accurate conversion rates, as rates can shift with new sales leaders or reps.
- Data often exported from CRM (e.g., Salesforce reports) for analysis, though native CRM reporting for conversion rates is sometimes available.
Sales Capacity Planning
- Model sales reps as "factories" that produce a certain number of deals or revenue (e.g., new business rep closes 4-5x their On-Target Earnings annually).
- Benchmarks for rep output vary by deal size: Enterprise ($50k+) ~1 deal/month; Mid-market ($5k-$15k) ~4-5 deals/month; Small deals ($1k-$2.5k) require high volume/fast close.
- Account for ramp-up periods (3-6 months typical, longer for enterprise, shorter for transactional) when building hiring plans for future revenue.
- Address seasonality by annualizing goals, or consider contract sales models for very low-touch, fast-training roles.
- Design commission plans to align with business cycles (e.g., bonuses after peak sales season like June/July) to mitigate post-payout turnover.
Advanced Lead Funnel Forecasting
- Complement sales capacity with detailed lead generation models (e.g., website visitors -> MQLs -> opportunities).
- Use a "cohorted" approach that recognizes delays and seasonality in lead conversion, rather than assuming immediate conversion.
- Track how long it takes for leads from specific cohorts (e.g., leads generated in January) to convert into opportunities over subsequent months.
- Achieves high accuracy (within 2-3% at $10M ARR) but demands significant data, effort, and analysis from various sources.
- Cohorts can be segmented by time, client type (e.g., district vs. school), or region (e.g., Texas vs. West Coast) for deeper insights. This approach is primarily for new business.
Investing in Customer Success & Data Ops
- Early-stage companies should "severely overinvest" in Customer Success to nail product-market fit and retention, ignoring initial cost benchmarks.
- Later-stage CSM benchmarks: Target $800k-$1M book of business per CSM. Large accounts (20-30 per CSM) versus small accounts (100-150 per CSM) require different engagement models.
- Design CSM compensation with high Net Revenue Retention targets (e.g., 140%), often with no variable payout until 100% NRR is achieved.
- For data integrity, hire a Sales Ops or TechOps specialist to manage CRM systems, automation, and reporting, initially as a part-time role.
- At scale, Sales Ops roles specialize into Systems (CRM, tech), Core Ops (headcount, strategy), and Learning & Development (training) teams.
