# Rethinking Curriculum in the Age of AI

Current State of Student Engagement
- Longitudinal data shows no meaningful change in student engagement since publishing “Doing School” over two decades ago.
- Student engagement defined by ABCs framework:
  - Affective: students enjoying/liking school
  - Behavioral: students doing the work/putting in effort
  - Cognitive: seeing meaning and purpose in learning
- Current engagement statistics (600 schools, 500k students globally):
  - Full engagement (all three ABCs): only 14% at high school level
  - “Doing school” (effort without joy/meaning): ~50% of students
  - Complete disengagement: 10-12%
- Academic stress remains top stressor for 20+ years across all socioeconomic levels
  - Grades, tests, quizzes, finals, workload, homework
  - Holds true from wealthy independent schools to under-resourced rural schools
AI Usage Patterns in Schools
- Student AI adoption nearly doubled year-over-year to ~80% admitting use
- Primary student uses:
  - Study help and understanding content
  - Summarizing materials
  - Generating/improving ideas for assignments
  - Very small percentage using for complete assignment generation
- Teacher adoption lags significantly (<50% vs 80% student usage)
- Major policy gap: many schools have no AI policies, creating confusion
- Students think AI should be allowed for learning support but not complete work replacement
- Academic dishonesty at 76% overall, with AI-when-prohibited climbing each year
Equity and Access Concerns
- Banning AI creates equity issues - resourced students get exposure outside school, while under-resourced students may only access internet/AI at school
- Students increasingly asked to demonstrate AI skills in job interviews
- Schools banning AI handicap students for real-world preparation
Purpose of Learning in AI Age
- Core question: what should schools teach when information is instantly accessible?
- Critical skills needed:
  - How to evaluate AI output quality vs hallucinations
  - Requires higher-level expertise to push back on AI responses
  - Social-emotional learning more important than ever
- Essential human capabilities:
  - Getting along with others
  - Critical thinking and nuance
  - Ethical behavior and moral citizenship
  - Face-to-face difficult conversations
- Concern about AI creating “generation of narcissists” through sycophantic responses
Curriculum Design Principles
- Must include student voice and choice
- Goal: real ownership and autonomy over learning
- Authentic assessment for real-world audiences
  - Examples: Writing editorials for newspapers (vs papers only teachers read), testing school water fountains vs just turning water blue
- Balance individual and collaborative work
  - Students want to work together, especially middle/high school
  - Avoid grading on curves that pit students against each other
- Just-right challenge zone (proximal development)
  - Not too easy (boring) or too hard (impossible)
  - May require differentiated materials per student
  - Professional development needed for implementation
Recommendations for Curriculum Builders
- Include teachers as end-users in design process - treat as professionals with expertise
- Shadow students full school day to understand real implementation context
- Curriculum construction can be “Trojan horse for school reform”
  - Good curriculum forces system changes (can’t do hands-on work in traditional 35-minute periods)
- Fight political pressures while surviving commercially
  - Use universal terms like “belonging” vs divisive buzzwords
  - Focus on what’s good for kids (unchanged developmental needs)
- Turn schools “inside out” - make extracurricular engagement the core learning model
- Address teacher preparation programs:
  - Break fear of technology/change
  - Deep understanding of AI algorithms
  - Rethink fundamental assumptions about homework, testing, memorization
