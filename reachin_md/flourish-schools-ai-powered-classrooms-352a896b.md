# Flourish Schools & AI-Powered Classrooms

Why John came back to building schools
- Didn't plan to start schools again after Rocketship. What changed his mind: watching AI tutoring tools get used only briefly in classrooms when the technology could support much more.
- Flourish started with a simple question: if AI can handle much of standards-based instruction, what should school look like if you design around that from scratch?
- That led to a broader bet: if core academics can become more automated, schools can spend much more of their energy on the things they’ve historically struggled to teach and measure well — especially human skills.
How Flourish thinks about the teacher's role
- At Flourish, the goal is not just to use AI tools inside a conventional school model. It’s to redefine the work of adults in schools.
- If schools don’t automate enough of standards-based instruction, teachers get pulled back into being instructional delivery machines. Flourish wants teachers focused more on coaching, relationships, and helping students grow in areas like agency and curiosity.
- That shift also changes hiring. Rocketship optimized much more for instructional execution. Flourish is looking more for adults who can connect with kids, motivate them, and support their development more holistically.
- This doesn’t mean abandoning rigor. In his view, K–5 academics still matter a lot, and Flourish has to ensure students are at or above grade level. The difference is that automation creates more room to focus on what comes next.
Why Flourish builds instead of buys software
- The economics of school software have changed. Flourish has been able to rebuild key workflows internally instead of relying on traditional vendors
- They replaced the portions of Seesaw they actually needed, especially around parent communication and messaging.
- They rebuilt their SIS after struggling with weak API access and slow integration work.
- His broader point is not just “build everything yourself.” It was that schools now have far more power than they used to. If a workflow matters and existing software is too rigid, a small technical team can increasingly solve it directly.
- The assumption that “this workflow is annoying, so schools will buy my SaaS” is weakening fast. Software companies will need deeper specialization, better automation, or some form of work product schools can’t easily recreate on their own.
- Systems need to become agent-aware and agent-friendly, not just API-accessible.
Measuring human skills with rigor
- Schools should treat human skills with the same seriousness they treat academics.
- Flourish tracks 24 “superpowers” — things like curiosity and agency — using classroom audio transcripts.
- AI rates students on 1–5 rubrics for each skill. Initial correlation with the lead teacher’s ratings was about 80%
- With repeated feedback on disagreements, the model improved to about 92%
- That has allowed Flourish to move toward a dashboard model where teachers can see where students may need support and respond more intentionally.
- A lot of existing SEL research is useful, but often not very measurable. Flourish is trying to treat that body of work more like a set of hypotheses to test.
Parent acquisition and community-based outreach
- Similar to the early Rocketship years: door-to-door outreach, church relationships, deep local trust-building, direct engagement with community networks (particularly mom groups)
- Microschools are already a compelling pitch for many families, especially those whose children feel lost in larger systems.
- The human-skills message also resonates, but only if paired with confidence that the school still takes grade-level academics seriously.
- Online acquisition can work well — and peers like Alpha and Primer rely heavily on it — but community embedding compounds over time in a way paid channels don’t. He compared it to SEO: hard work up front, slow payoff, but valuable once it starts working.
Following ESAs in Arizona and Florida
- Universal ESA states are the easiest place to start microschools
- Not just about policy, but also about culture and market readiness:
  - In Arizona and Florida, families already understand the concept
  - In Tennessee, much more education and explanation is required
  - That difference materially changes speed of growth
    Texas came up too. John suggested Flourish will likely be there eventually, but he remains cautious because of prior regulatory experience from Rocketship.
Technology positioning amid edtech backlash and tech concerns
- Flourish doesn’t market itself as an “AI school.” John’s view is that parents care about outcomes, not whether the infrastructure runs on AI. Tech is meant to stay in the background.
- Don’t confuse automation with the end goal. For Flourish, automation matters because it creates room for a different kind of school.
- Longer-term hope that schools may eventually move beyond screens more than deeper into them — for example, toward lightweight voice-based interfaces instead of laptop-heavy experiences.
- Unapologetic that AI should be part of how students learn. The “problem of the day” example captured this well: students use AI, YouTube, and other tools to solve unfamiliar advanced problems.
- The point is not perfect mastery of the underlying field in one sitting. The point is learning how to make progress when you don’t already know the answer.
Structure, rigor, and where Flourish differs from Alpha
- Gentle critique of the Alpha message that if academics get compressed into a few hours, kids should simply have the rest of the day open.
- Schools should be intentional and evidence-seeking about how they develop agency, curiosity, and other human skills — not just assume those outcomes emerge automatically from looser structure.
ESA, equity, and having parents as the customer
- ESA-funded schooling is a very different private-school model than the traditional elite version many people associate with the sector.
- When funding is universal and schools are trying to grow, the incentives shift:
  - schools are motivated to serve more families, not create scarcity
  - family demand matters more than political approval
  - the model can look more like charter growth logic than legacy private-school exclusivity
- Flourish currently takes all applicants.
  - The biggest caveat is around students with more intensive special needs, where they may not yet have the staffing or support to serve every family well.
- Approach to serving more diverse communities: Putting a school on the border between a wealthier and lower-income neighborhood.
