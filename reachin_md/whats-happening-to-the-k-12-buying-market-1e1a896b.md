01:02: Current Trends in K-12 Education Funding: A look at the current state of K-12 funding, currently around $800 billion. Spending has been growing faster than inflation, but student performance, as indicated by NAEP scores, has been declining. This trend raises concerns about the sustainability of funding increases, especially as federal relief funds have ended.
02:43 - Challenges Facing School Districts including tighter budgets, declining enrollment, and increased costs in special education. With many districts experiencing financial strain, there is a renewed focus on improving student outcomes while managing costs effectively.
08:12 - Teacher Shortage and Retention: Marguerite asserts that the teacher shortage is effectively over, as many districts are no longer hiring due to budget constraints and a lack of job openings. While there was a surge in hiring during the influx of ESSER funds, many districts are actually now hoping for attrition among newer teachers to manage staffing levels. (This doesn’t mean there are enough qualified teachers, particularly in math and special ed.)
15:01 - Impact of Staffing on Budgets: How increased staffing levels are locking up budgets, making it difficult for districts to allocate funds for non-labor expenses. Marguerite emphasizes that as districts face tighter budgets, purchasing will likely decline, affecting vendors and service providers.
20:31 - Opportunities in Special Education: Optimism about the potential for technology and data tools to improve efficiency in special education. She notes that special education costs are rising, and there is a growing need for innovative solutions to manage these expenses while meeting federal requirements.
29:43 - Education Savings Accounts (ESAs): Still a drop in the overall bucket of funding, but as families use funds for private schooling or educational services, it could shift some financial resources away from traditional public schools.
43:15 - English Language Learners and the possible impact from federal legislation around immigration and demographic trends.
45:19 - Federal programs (Title I, IDEA) unlikely to change significantly, due to the Congressional motions needed to effect any major changes, and from what Trump and McMahon have shared publicly.
49:22 - Selling schools on ways to upskill their staff what catchphrases and keywords to use/not use.
53:34 - Concerns about federal data collection efforts? Not really - NCES data is often outdated and slow; Edunomics has gone to the states directly for more reliable and timely data. NAEP will probably continue.
