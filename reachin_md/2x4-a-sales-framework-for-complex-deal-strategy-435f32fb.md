Complex Deals vs. Simple Sales
- Founders are inherently skilled at one-on-one sales (e.g., fundraising, hiring), but complex deals differ significantly.
- Complex deals involve multiple parties with diverse motivations, varied decision-making processes, and extended timelines (potentially years).
- The "people complexity" requires extensive discovery to understand each player and their individual processes.
- For small B2B sales, a single person might be the user, decider, and purchaser, leading to shorter sales cycles (even 90 days can be long).
Sales Playbook Pillars
- Ideal Customer Profile (ICP): Define your ideal customer to guide qualification questions, distinct from marketing segmentation.
- Your Story (3-3-30): Prepare 30-second (elevator pitch), 3-minute (deeper dive), and 30-minute (first meeting) versions.
- Stages & Gates: Outline the sales process from initial contact (SQL) through subsequent qualification steps.
- Qualification & Discovery: Use frameworks like BANT to determine if a prospect can actually buy your product.
- Demo: Provide a demonstration to substantiate your product claims and show problem-solving.
- Objection Handling: Treat every question as an objection; write down and have consistent, strong answers for your team.
- Documentation: Pull all playbook elements (ICP, story, stages, questions, objections) out of your head and into a shared document (Google Doc, spreadsheet) for scalability.
The 2x4 Framework Overview
- A simplified sales framework designed to aid in complex selling by mapping your situation across two dimensions: People and Process.
- This framework is applicable to complex customer sales and channel partnerships.
- It identifies four key personas in complex deals: Joey the Champion, Phoebe the Buyer, Ross the Advocate, and Monica the Blocker.
Joey the Champion
- Definition: Someone who voluntarily takes personal risk and interest in the adoption, implementation, and success of your product within their organization. They "fight for you when you're not in the room."
- Identification: High responsiveness, sharing direct contact (cell), revealing internal budget cycles, org structures, and personality quirks, creative problem-solving.
- Importance: Essential for complex deals; winning without a champion relies on luck. They act as your internal voice and leverage.
- Pro-tips: Use them for deal status updates, as an organizational point person, encourage "we" language, and cultivate multiple champions across departments.
- Red Flag: If you lose your champion, seriously consider the deal dead for that cycle, or an existing customer at risk of being "booted." You will likely need to restart the sales process with a new internal contact.
Phoebe the Buyer
- Definition: The decision-making authority who ultimately pays for the product; distinct from the champion.
- Meeting the Buyer: Crucial for complex deals. While the champion influences, the buyer needs to know you, understand what problems you solve, and trust you.
- Buyer Priorities: Focus on their core problems ("what is keeping you up at night?") and assessing the risk of engaging with a startup (e.g., "Are you going to be around in a year?").
- Startup Advantage: Leverage your small size to build trust by providing direct access and rapid, high-level problem resolution when issues arise.
- Complexity: The buyer may not be a single person; it could be a committee or involve multiple signatories (e.g., business, security, legal decision-makers). Ask directly: "How does this process work? Who actually makes the decision? Who signs the contract?"
Process: Protocols vs. Rituals
- Protocols: Uniform, explicit, and often written organizational requirements (e.g., "sign an NDA," "talk to Infosec," "procurement only reviews deals in April").
- Rituals: Unwritten, departmental, or individual habitual practices (e.g., "we submit our budget in December, get approval in March, so let's not meet until April"). These can vary across departments.
- Customer Discovery: Crucial for uncovering both explicit protocols and unwritten rituals to understand the full buying process.
- Failure Mode: Avoid starting with your product. Instead, prioritize deep customer discovery to understand their problems and ensure a genuine solution-to-problem fit before proceeding.
