BizOps Reflects Company Gaps
- Most functions have clear mandates (e.g., Product builds, Sales sells), but BizOps is hard to define, often described as project management, data analytics, or "jack of all trades."
- The true mandate of BizOps is to *find and fill company gaps*, and these common descriptions are often temporary symptoms.
- Companies frequently hire for the symptom (e.g., a data person) rather than the root cause (e.g., functional teams lack data analysis skills).
- Risk of hiring for symptoms: talent becomes suboptimized (e.g., generic athlete stuck doing specific data cleanup) or too specific (e.g., SQL expert stuck when the gap shifts).
- Self-assessment: Ask "What specific gap is my BizOps team *really* filling?" (e.g., executive capability, functional underperformance, data analysis deficit).
- Practical Example: Joe took over the website relaunch because Marketing "weren't good" at it, but the goal is to fix it, build the capability, and embed ownership back into Marketing.
Two Paths to High-Value BizOps
- Type 1: Time Savings ROI (Tactical/Leverage): Focus on optimizing people's time and velocity in early-stage companies (Seed to Series B/PMF).
- Examples: building sales enablement, data cleanup, standardizing contracts, setting up infrastructure to provide leverage.
- Model: Hire junior, mentally flexible talent; "launch and move on" (don't hold tasks long-term).
- Pros: Easy to start, high early impact, but maxes out due to diminishing marginal returns (e.g., salesperson utilization caps at 100%).
- Type 2: Zero to One / X-Factor ROI (Strategic): Focus on projects that can 2X, 5X, 10X the business and multiply equity in larger, VC-backed companies (Series B to IPO).
- Examples: 500% website conversion improvement, launching new industries, M&A, new product revenue models. Joe's team at Clary is designed for this.
- Model: Hire senior talent for high-inertia problems; "launch and move on" to avoid getting stuck.
- Quote: "The only thing that matters is multiplying equity." Cost optimization or procurement efforts typically don't move the needle enough for venture-backed businesses.
Avoiding Low-Impact Pitfalls
- "Cool Project Graveyard": BizOps with fuzzy mandates often take on "additive" projects (e.g., tech consolidation, training optimization, indirect spend analysis) that don't sufficiently multiply equity for venture-backed companies.
- This work is valuable for Fortune 500s but can be a waste of time and salary in a startup seeking hypergrowth. Joe routinely says "no" to such requests (e.g., company intranet).
- "Trains and Planes ROI": Focusing on general project management, KPIs, reporting, or continuous improvement, often seen in large, siloed organizations (e.g., 18-month ERP rollouts).
- While valuable for managing complexity in Fortune 500s, these long-term, process-heavy projects are too slow and typically not high-impact enough for fast-paced VC-backed businesses.
- Advice: Pick one end of the BizOps spectrum (Tactical or Strategic) or hybridize (e.g., 80/20 split) rather than getting stuck in the low-impact middle.
- Regularly ask: "Are we working on the most important thing right now?" to ensure alignment with equity multiplication.
Prioritizing Projects & People
- Build soft power: BizOps lacks hard power (direct reporting lines), so effectiveness hinges on building a reputation for getting things done and earning trust.
- Project selection: Initially, pick shorter projects (2-3 months) to quickly show progress and build personal "brand equity" to tackle longer, higher-stakes initiatives later.
- Idea generation: Source projects from both internal "wish lists" (listening to pain points) and your own observations, then filter based on your team's mandate (leverage or equity multiplication).
- Resource allocation: Joe's team typically splits 30-40% on ongoing work and 60-70% on discrete projects.
- For ongoing work (e.g., competitive team, moonshots), the BizOps role is to *launch, build the foundation, teach, and then guide*, embedding capability within the relevant functional team rather than owning it indefinitely.
- Using external help: For major lifts (e.g., full website revamp), consider paying agencies for speed and expertise, then bring ongoing maintenance in-house when costs are lower.
Evolving Your Function & Career
- BizOps roles *should* be a stepping stone to leadership (e.g., GM of a launched product/project/geo) or functional ownership, but a clear C-suite path beyond COO is less obvious.
- Transitioning from tactical to strategic BizOps: First, build a strong personal reputation and trust by consistently delivering on tactical projects.
- Second, proactively engage leadership to explicitly *change the team's mandate* from infrastructure/leverage to equity multiplication, supported by your track record.
- Frame the conversation around maximizing equity: "Which option (A or B) do you think leads to the most amount of equity for this business?" This aligns incentives and justifies saying no to lower-impact work.
- Talent development: Cultivate a diverse skill set: baseline analysis/storytelling, "trains and planes" (project management, communication), and "hunt and kill" (diagnose, fix, launch new initiatives).
- Avoid becoming too specialized (e.g., "only data person"), as the core of BizOps is filling diverse gaps.
