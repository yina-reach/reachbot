Strategos background
- Strategos team made up of former senators, legislators, commissioners of education, educators, and teachers who advise education companies on go-to-market, business development, government affairs, and market intelligence.
- Combine qualitative insight (statehouse relationships, podcast platform, operator experience) with a proprietary data layer of ~729,000 data points on policy and funding.
K-12 market: Shift to a state-led era of funding
- Historically, federal government has functioned like a bank with guidelines while states implemented within those guardrails
- With ESSER ending and the Department of Education in transition, there is growing emphasis on workforce, choice, and funds flowing through multiple agencies.
- Expects states to gain more power and discretion through waivers and shifting responsibilities, which will push companies from a single multi-state strategy to 35–40 state-specific strategies
- Founders should think through three dynamics:
  - Legacy revenue: how exposed your existing revenue is to federal or state funds and whether you’re mispricing that risk.
  - State relationships: SEAs in many small/mid-sized states now have more autonomy and choice than they did during ESSER.
  - Concentration: your revenue concentration in politically salient states now matters much more than it did a few years ago.
State budget landscape
- FY26 turned out better than headlines suggested. Many states increased or at least held K‑12 funding in nominal dollars, even where the environment felt tough.
- A few states struggled to finalize budgets (e.g., Michigan, Pennsylvania, North Carolina), and North Carolina still lacked a final budget at the time of the analysis.
- The Inflation Reduction Act and changes to federal entitlements forced midstream adjustments in some states (e.g., Colorado, Oregon).
- Looking ahead to FY27:
  - Blue, high-tax states are facing structural deficits and more budget headwinds than red states.
  - Red states often show rising spend, but much of the new money is earmarked for choice programs rather than traditional district channels.
Elections, governors, and why leadership turnover matters
- 36 states will hold gubernatorial and/or state legislative elections, with ~24 new governors expected across cycles.
- Even when party control does not change, a new governor will bring new priorities and pet projects.
- In about seven “true toss-up” purple states, control could flip, driving sharp shifts in policy and funding priorities.
  - Example: Arizona shifted from a Republican to a Democratic governor while retaining a Republican legislature, triggering major fights over school choice.
- Founders should factor governor timelines and lame-duck periods into state plans, especially where budget deficits and leadership change overlap (e.g., Colorado).
Federal funding: flat but still flowing
- Core federal education funding (e.g., Title I, IDEA) has been flat for three straight years (FY24–26) and is likely to stay flat into FY27
- Some Department of Education staff and programs have shifted toward the Department of Labor, but the big formula programs and their funding streams are largely unaffected.
- Attempts to hold back or reshape smaller discretionary programs have generally failed due to political or legal pushback.
- Roughly 95% of federal dollars are expected to continue flowing down the same historical channels; state waivers will change implementation details more than the direction of funds.
How politics shapes issues and messaging
- Some issues are strongly partisan, requiring smart, tailored messaging
  - In Florida, “SEL” is radioactive language, but “mental health,” “persistence,” and similar concepts are acceptable, even when the goals overlap.
- Some areas enjoy durable bipartisan support:
  - Early childhood education attracts sustained interest and funding across parties.
  - College, career, and workforce readiness, including CTE, is high priority in nearly every state, with relatively low political risk.
- Founders should localize language and positioning by state, even when selling the exact same product and delivery model. Should appear as neutral as possible.
  - Website copy, blog posts, social feeds can trigger pushback and derail multimillion-dollar opportunities
“Flags vs. forts” model
- Their “flags and forts” approach:
  - Invest (flags): States with aligned leadership, budgets, and active conversations on your issue. Aim to plant a flag and build.
  - Protect (forts): States where you already have material revenue. Competitors will actively try to displace you; advocacy is insurance.
  - Monitor: States with no near-term (6–12 month) opening but enough potential to keep someone on a light monitoring contract.
- Many companies behave like firefighters, chasing crises around the map. A flags/forts/monitor framework pulls them toward proactive, portfolio-level planning.
Formulating state strategy: focus, density, and replication
- Focus on 5–7 high-impact states where conditions are favorable and where success travels (e.g., Florida as a bellwether on center-right policy).
- Build density in those states so that contracts, champions, and outcomes reinforce each other.
- Treat those states as reference points that can be replicated in similarly aligned states via legislator networks and conferences (e.g., ALEC, NCSL).
- Timelines matter: Bills often take multiple sessions to pass, with progress and stalls along the way.
  - Founders should be realistic about how long it takes to go from interest to policy to funded program.
- Resource allocation:
  - Managing 25–35 state engagements is expensive and operationally complex unless you are prepared to spend heavily on government affairs every month.
  - Legislators like to replicate policies they see working elsewhere, so concentrated wins can open doors beyond your initial footprint.
AI and how to talk about technology with states
- Educate decision-makers on what your AI does and how it works. If you are vague, they will often assume the worst.
  - Distinguish clearly between administrative AI (e.g., supporting teachers and staff, driving efficiency) and child-facing AI.
- On technology in classrooms:
  - Strong backlash in some states against screen time, 1:1 devices, and child-facing technology, especially in early childhood and K‑5.
  - In Florida and similar states, bills have repeatedly surfaced to ban most child-facing technology in early years, even while interest in AI’s potential for instruction remains.
  - Be explicit about safety, guardrails, and instructional value, especially in early childhood contexts.
