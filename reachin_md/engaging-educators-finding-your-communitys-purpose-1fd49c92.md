Audience vs. True Community
- Audience are people you talk to and listen to; Engagement refers to metrics like likes/comments, which can move users up a "commitment curve" (e.g., a like < a comment < a share < befriending).
- A network connects people with a common interest to receive something, but lacks deeper relational ties (e.g., the Discovery Educator Network initially functioned as a network).
- A true community requires members to have feelings of fellowship, joint ownership, and shared commitment, existing with or without the brand's direct presence.
- Avoid a "mirage community" where people claim community but lack genuine care and connection for one another.
- Members of a true community integrate it into their identity, often expressing belonging at events and in social interactions (Identity Theory).
- Communities are best for building deeper relationships with existing audiences; they are not primarily designed for audience acquisition.
Loyalty: Your Community's Purpose
- The single, solid objective of building a community is to increase loyalty among users and customers.
- Community building is a long-term commitment, not an immediate gratification marketing effort; it takes time to yield results.
- Benefits include improved product (via user input), increased retention, deeper mission alignment, and eventually customer expansion and purchases.
- It is crucial to pick one primary purpose for your community; attempting to achieve everything at once will dilute its effectiveness and strategic focus.
The SPACES Framework Defined
- S - Customer Support: Members answer each other's technical questions and solve problems, offering the easiest measurable return on investment (e.g., Google Classroom Help, Fitbit community). Often seen organically in unmanaged Facebook groups.
- P - Product Feedback: Gathers input for product ideation and future development (e.g., Lego Ideas). This is rarely a community's *sole* purpose but can be a valuable feature.
- A - Acquisition & Advocacy: Cultivates ambassadors who drive awareness and growth for the business (e.g., the Discovery Educator Network's evolution). This requires deep, authentic relationships and give-and-take, unlike transactional referral programs.
- C - Content Contribution: Increases successful contributions of user-generated content like lesson plans, code, or resources (e.g., Breakout EDU, Teachable). Few education brands focus solely on this.
- E - Engagement: Builds a community around a shared interest that is *broader than your product*, openly allowing discussion of competitors. This demonstrates authenticity and positions the brand as a contributor to the wider topic (e.g., PBL Works/BUCK Institute).
Customer Success: EdTech's Primary Goal
- The Customer Success (S) community type is considered the "winner winner chicken dinner" and is the most common in EdTech (80%+ of brands).
- It connects customers to share best practices, driving usage, retention, product adoption, and eventual customer expansion.
- This type of community naturally incorporates other SPACES purposes like product feedback and advocacy, allowing for a comprehensive approach.
- Focusing on Customer Success provides the flexibility to meet varied member needs while maintaining a clear, strategic objective for leadership.
Strategic Community Foundations
- Define your community's purpose by identifying the "magic in the middle," an overlap in a Venn diagram of your Business Objectives, Member Needs (discovered by listening), and Company Capabilities (unique offerings you can provide).
- Clearly articulate your community's primary focus to leadership; once active, various departments (e.g., engineering for product testing) will want a piece, so define what it *will* and *will not* do.
- In EdTech, carefully consider which stakeholder your community is built for (users, buyers, influencers), as users are often not the direct purchasers, which impacts purpose and strategy.
- Ultimately, a community's primary purpose is to improve the lives of its members, which also re-energizes your own team and validates your mission.
Start Small, Cultivate Culture
- Start small and don't be a perfectionist: Begin by identifying 5-10 "biggest advocates" or people who deeply love your brand; invite them to collaborate and help launch, as they often feel honored to be involved.
- Cultivate culture by modeling desired behavior: Actively participate and demonstrate the interactions you wish to see (e.g., "I see you, I'm here to support you"), then celebrate others who adopt these behaviors to set the tone.
- Empathy is a key "tool": Community building is a human-centric, co-creative, and non-linear process, contrasting with more predictable social media engagement metrics.
- While various platforms exist (e.g., Facebook Groups, Slack, Mighty Networks, Discord, Circle, Edmodo), the choice of platform is less critical than the commitment and intentionality of building relationships.
- Communities evolve through a life cycle: "critical mass" occurs when members contribute more than staff, and "mitosis" (splitting into new communities) can happen when different audience segments develop distinct needs.
