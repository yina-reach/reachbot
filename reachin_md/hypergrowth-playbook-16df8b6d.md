Navigating Hypergrowth Challenges
- Hypergrowth means constantly identifying and fixing "what is breaking" across the organization.
- Treat shared insights as "one data point," not "the truth," to inform decisions for your unique company.
- Consider hiring a first Head of People around 50 employees, as logistics (offer letters, PEOs, international hires) and culture become critical breaking points.
Performance & Purposeful Exits
- Formal performance evaluations (e.g., 5-point scale ratings) were introduced around 120-150 people when role clarity improved.
- Design processes for development and growth of the 90-95% of strong performers, rather than focusing on underperformers.
- Make quick decisions on non-fits; "don't drag it out" as toxic impact amplifies. Offer generous exit packages to support transitions.
- Use OKRs for goal clarity (where to spend time, mission), but don't strictly link to performance reviews due to rapid company changes; focus on qualities like adaptability and impact.
Strategic Talent Acquisition
- Use external search firms for critical hires if under-resourced or if internal recruiters are overwhelmed (e.g., 18-20 reqs/recruiter leads to "shoveling candidates" not sourcing).
- Aim for 10-12 reqs per internal recruiter to allow for full ownership: strategic sourcing, first-level screening, weekly hiring manager updates, and pipeline strategy.
- "Always be raising the bar of talent"; involve executive leadership in the interview process (up to ~100 people) to prevent junior managers from hiring only more junior staff.
- For early-stage companies, most hires (60-70%) come from referrals; rely on hiring managers to remember and keep in touch with standout candidates.
Evolving Leadership & Structure
- At any stage, ensure ~50% of the executive team has experience from a company "two years advanced" to guide future growth and define what "good looks like."
- Embrace the "Give Away Your Legos" mindset; leaders should be willing to transition out of old roles as the company outgrows their experience for that specific position.
- Address resistance to organizational changes (e.g., new reporting lines) by first presenting clear business logic, then tackling underlying emotional concerns (ego, status, access).
- Implement RACI or RAPID frameworks to clarify roles and responsibilities, normalizing a "quick two minutes" at the start of any project to define who is accountable, consulted, or informed.
Cultivating Resilient Culture
- Focus on building your company's cultural DNA when small (15-100 people) by democratically asking employees what they see, hear, and feel.
- Define authentic, core values that become deeply embedded in daily language and operations; simplify if too numerous (e.g., from 16 to 4 values).
- Values can evolve (e.g., refresh every few years if a value feels "outgrown"), but they should remain a consistent "north star," not change quarterly like OKRs.
- Prioritize "creating space" for human connection and dialogue (e.g., starting team meetings with personal struggles); solving human relationship issues (anxiety, ego) is foundational for business success.
Optimize Communication & Feedback
- Tackle "Slack noise" through regular etiquette education (prioritization, tagging, channel organization) for new hires and existing staff; consider upgrading Slack infrastructure for better sorting.
- Move non-critical chatter out of business communication channels (e.g., dedicated "chatter channels," internal podcast platforms).
- Foster a feedback culture by investing in teaching employees *how to solicit feedback* from anyone, rather than solely relying on managers to give it.
- When seeking input, ask for "advice" ("What tips would you give me for doing it better next time?") instead of "feedback" to encourage more honest and actionable responses.
