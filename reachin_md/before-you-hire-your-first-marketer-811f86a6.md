Understanding Marketing's Core Value
- Marketing is a critical business function driving the customer journey from awareness to purchase, not just "pretty colors" or tactical tasks.
- What marketing cares about (e.g., share of voice, pipeline, conversions) should align with what the entire company cares about.
- Measure marketing's effectiveness through share of voice, inbound interest, pipeline opportunities, and purchase conversion rates.
- Focus on efficient pipeline generation, measuring pipeline created relative to investment made.
Navigating the Modern Marketing Landscape
- Recognize that over 60% of buyers, even in B2B, conduct research and make decisions online without engaging sales; your digital presence must earn trust.
- Adapt to a privacy-first, cookie-less world (e.g., GDPR, US state laws) by enabling customers to self-identify and provide value without intrusive data collection.
- Prioritize brand consistency across all touchpoints (messaging, visuals, user experience) as consistent brands generate one-third more revenue.
- Understand that the end-to-end customer experience is as important as, if not more than, the underlying product or service itself.
Structuring Your First Marketing Hires
- Be open to serendipitous hires of "special people" even if they don't perfectly fit a predefined role.
- Consider a bottom-up hiring order: Product Marketing first, then Revenue Marketing, followed by Corporate Marketing.
- Product Marketing is foundational, focusing on deep customer understanding, positioning, messaging, and sales enablement; good PMMs often cover other marketing areas initially.
- Revenue Marketing drives demand generation, pipeline, and operational measurement; Corporate Marketing manages brand, awareness, and public relations.
- Leverage agencies (e.g., digital demand generation) to fill specific skill gaps or execute campaigns before making full-time hires.
Key Marketing Roles & Deliverables
- **Product Marketing:** Delivers precise customer profiles identifying high-pain dynamics (targeting 75%+ close rates), crisp and consistent core messaging, and targeted content that avoids jargon for clarity (e.g., Asana's "Teamwork for all your work").
- **Revenue Marketing:** Builds pipeline models (forecasting ARR, conversion rates, sales cycle timing – even if imperfect initially), runs targeted programs (targeting, message, art, offer), and manages metrics. Strive for interactive, clear, and engaging campaigns (e.g., Desmos).
- **Corporate Marketing:** Defines brand identity and voice, creates awareness (PR, analyst relations), and fosters customer advocacy. A strong, consistent brand (e.g., Progressive's Dr. Rick) leads to more efficient sales and predisposed customers.
- Implement "laddered" messaging: a core, high-level message with persona-specific or buying-stage-specific messages that support it.
Founder's Checklist: Before You Hire
- **Prepare for a long haul:** Hiring a Head of Marketing takes months of searching and several months in the seat *before* a major launch or critical event.
- **Know your company's core value:** Hire a marketer who can appreciate and amplify your existing strengths, rather than just complementing weaknesses.
- **Prioritize culture & product alignment:** Seek a marketer who will deeply integrate with your company culture and understand the product as well as engineers or product managers.
- **Value qualities over credentials:** Focus on personality traits like curiosity, "chutzpah," relentlessness, high emotional intelligence (EQ), a drive for excellence, and honesty over specific industry experience or years in a role.
- **Anticipate investment:** Your first marketer will need resources to build a team and run experiments; some early "waste" is an expected part of learning ROI.
- **Commit fully to brand:** Brand consistency is a company-wide endeavor, not solely marketing's responsibility; be prepared to make a whole-company investment.
Effective Interviewing & Vetting
- Ask "What have you done in the past year that you're proud of?" to uncover depth and insight.
- Apply the "Five Whys" to their answer: dig into the *how*, what worked/didn't, who they had to convince, what went wrong, and what they learned.
- Trust your instincts from interviews, but always verify with thorough background checks to gain a complete picture of their capabilities and character.
