Why Monetization Matters
- Monetization is an underutilized lever with a 4x greater impact on the bottom line than top-of-funnel acquisition, according to ProfitWell data from 500+ SaaS companies.
- The core principle is matching price to value, ensuring fair compensation for the long-term value created for customers.
- Founders often underprice initially to remove friction and gain users, leaving significant money on the table.
- Pricing assumptions are rarely revisited, despite compounding product value over time.
Strategic Segmentation & Perceived Value
- Move beyond demographic segmentation to behavioral: understand what customers truly need, value, and are willing to pay for.
- Uber effectively segments its offering (Pool, X, SUV, Black) based on user needs (price, speed, space, comfort) and willingness to pay.
- Value ≠ perceived value; a long list of features can cause "feature shock" if users don't understand or need them.
- A company for SMBs removed 19 of 27 features, increased price, and saw a 25% lift in sales by increasing perceived value.
- Answer these key questions: Who is your core segment? Why do they pay? Which specific levers drive conversion/upgrade? How much are they willing to pay?
A Practical Pricing Process
- Assign an owner: A dedicated task force (e.g., growth product team: PMs, data science, design) should own pricing and revisit assumptions regularly.
- Qualitative research: Foster internal feedback loops with customer operations (sales, success, support) for "voice of the user." Utilize surveys like conjoint, MaxDiff (must-have/nice-to-have), and Van Westendorp.
- Van Westendorp Method: Ask customers for acceptable, expensive-but-still-pay, prohibitively expensive, and too-cheap-it-questions-quality price points to understand willingness-to-pay breakpoints.
- Quantitative experimentation: Don't hesitate to raise prices; backlash is often less than anticipated. Experiment with price changes, isolating by geography if possible (e.g., Australia before US/Canada).
- Ongoing evolution: Pricing is never "done." Revisit price points every 6 months to ensure alignment with compounding product value.
- If sales conversions are very high with no price pushback, you are likely leaving money on the table; use sales conversations to test higher price points.
- For existing B2B customers: Announce price increases well in advance, offer a chance to renew at the old price for a limited time (drives expansion/retention), and always marry price increases with an increase in *perceived* value (new features, benefits).
Tactical Monetization Levers: Sticks & Carrots
- Sticks (Value Metrics/Quota Limits): Features that force an upgrade when a user hits a limit (e.g., Dropbox storage, Slack messages, New York Times articles). Highly effective as they catch users in a high-intent state with clear value.
- Carrots (Premium Features): New functionality dangled to entice users to upgrade.
- The best monetization strategies effectively marry both sticks and carrots.
- Carta's "Stick" Example: Initially considered admin seats or equity grants, but landed on number of shareholders as the value metric, directly scaling with the complexity of cap table management and perceived value.
- Carta's "Carrot" Example: Product roadmap aligned with customer growth, building complex features (secondaries, IPOs, international) for growing companies and charging more accordingly.
Personalized Monetization with Data
- For high-volume, high-velocity businesses, use predictive data (ML models) to segment users by propensity to convert, upgrade, or renew.
- Invoice2Go Example: Doubled upgrade rate and increased upgrade price without backlash by personalizing the experience.
- Operationalize ML models:
- For users with *low propensity to renew*, reinforce core features, build awareness of underused benefits, or offer targeted promotions.
- For users with *high propensity to renew*, educate them on features in the next plan up during renewal, driving significant expansion ARR.
Pricing Page & Multi-Tier Strategy
- Consumer products with single pricing (Evernote Example): A single price point ($45/year) was a mistake, as low-value users found it too high, while high-value users (daily, "priceless" archive) would have paid $200-300. Bifurcation is crucial to capture value from different segments.
- Website pricing:
- For self-serve businesses where customers can swipe a card, publish prices.
- For sales-led businesses requiring hand-holding/implementation, "Contact Us" is appropriate.
- Consider a hybrid: publish a lower price for entry-level segments and use "Contact Us" for higher tiers, differentiating by feature sets or quota limits, not just internal segmentation like "public vs. private schools."
- While multiple plans add complexity, the revenue maximization often outweighs it. Guide users with clear lead-ins (e.g., "This plan is for companies that are X, Y, Z...").
- Limit the number of "sticks" or value metrics per plan to avoid "analysis paralysis" or a "Chinese menu" effect.
Aligning Product & Monetization Teams
- Product roadmap and monetization are two sides of the same coin.
- Early-stage companies should prioritize demand research (what do people want?) followed by willingness-to-pay research (how much will they pay?) to inform both product and pricing.
- Consistent customer conversations (existing and prospective) are invaluable; avoid building for "ourselves" and embrace visceral feedback.
- Strong growth Product Managers can often encompass the scope of product marketing, especially in smaller organizations, by owning pricing, customer empathy, and managing all owned and operated customer touchpoints.
