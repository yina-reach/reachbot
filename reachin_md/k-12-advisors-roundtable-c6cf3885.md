10:40  Advice to Edtech Founders in a Time of Budget (ESSER) Constraints
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Multi-year Contracts: Focus on securing long-term deals, even if districts can only commit to one year at a time. This can provide stability and predictability for both parties.
- Pricing Negotiations: Be open to negotiating prices. Understand that districts are under significant financial pressure due to the ESSER cliff.
- Patience and Flexibility: Recognize that districts are navigating complex financial landscapes and may need time to make decisions.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Outcomes-based Contracting: Shift towards contracts that focus on student outcomes rather than just services. This shows confidence in your product’s effectiveness.
  - Important resource: [Southern Education Foundation’s Outcomes-based Contracting Toolkit](https://obc.southerneducation.org/resources/#toolkit)
- Strategic Development: Be strategic about the features you develop. Avoid unnecessary functionalities like dashboards that bigger districts may already have robust systems in place.
- Evidence-based Practices: Provide independent research and evidence to support the efficacy of your product. This can be a strong selling point.
Priti Johari (Milpitas Unified)
- Outcomes-based Contracting: Engage in conversations about outcomes-based contracting. This approach ties payment to the achievement of specific metrics.
- Partnerships with Teachers and Unions: Show how your product adds value to classroom teachers and is not just an outsourcing tool. This can help in gaining broader acceptance.
- Integration with Existing Platforms: Ensure that your services can be easily integrated with other major systems that districts already use. This increases usability and adoption.
Ty Gorman (Barrington 220)
- Avoid Large Price Increases: Do not implement significant price hikes, especially if they are to cover new features that the district may not use.
- Customization and Responsiveness: Be responsive to the specific needs of the district, but avoid unnecessary new features that complicate the product. Focus on what you do best.
- Long-term Relationships: Focus on building long-term relationships rather than short-term gains. This can lead to more sustainable partnerships.
Babak Mostaghimi  (Learner Studio, formerly Gwinnett County Public Schools)
- Complement Existing Priorities: Ensure that your tools complement the district’s existing priorities and do not add unnecessary new capacities.
- Market Insights: Share insights and white papers that can influence the creation of RFPs to better align with what your product offers.
- Avoid Overpromising: Be realistic about what your product can achieve and avoid adding features that do not directly address the district’s core needs.
25:00  Teacher and Staffing Challenges
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Long-term and Short-term Approaches: Fayette County is taking both long-term and short-term approaches to address staffing challenges. Long-term efforts include reworking the pipeline for teachers and staff in collaboration with local universities.
- Master Schedules: In the short term, the district is closely analyzing master schedules to optimize staffing. They are bringing in experts to assist with this complex process.
- Increased Salaries: Fayette County has raised salaries to retain staff. The district now has the highest minimum salary in the state of Kentucky, which has helped retain staff and reduce vacancies. Currently, the district has only 20 vacancies out of 70 campuses, which is considered a good position. But the challenge is that the quality is lacking.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Trade-off: We are fully staffed on school psychologists, which is unheard of. We are doing well on staffing, but we are paying for them. This competes with innovations, new products — this is the hard tradeoff.
28:30  Strategies and Priorities for Parental Involvement and Engagement
Ty Gorman (Barrington 220)
- Responsibility Balance: There is an ongoing conversation about the balance of responsibilities between parents and the school. Parents want schools to take on certain roles but also do not want to be replaced in their parental duties. This dynamic shapes how the district approaches parent engagement and involvement. The community has a back-and-forth about where the line is drawn — this is happening nationally.
Priti Johari (Milpitas Unified)
- LCAP Surveys: In California, districts are required to complete the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP), which mandates parent engagement. Milpitas Unified uses interactive surveys through ThoughtExchange to gather feedback from parents, teachers, and students. Over 5,000 participants provided input on district priorities.
- Committee Involvement: The district involves parents in committees to develop the LCAP. These committees review evidence-based practices and help narrow down potential interventions. This collaborative approach ensures that parents have a say in the decision-making process and that the selected solutions meet the needs of the community.
- Product Evaluation: Parents are also involved in evaluating potential products and services. Priti presents web pages and information about vendors to parent committees, allowing them to explore and provide feedback on potential solutions.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Centralized Support Hub: Chelsea highlighted the importance of addressing non-academic needs to support families. APS is opening a centralized support hub to help families navigate challenges related to housing, food insecurity, clothing, and other basic needs. This approach aims to improve student attendance and engagement by ensuring that families feel supported and valued.
- Nontraditional Support: The district is exploring nontraditional ways to support parents, such as offering free sports physicals. By addressing a broader range of family needs, APS hopes to create a more supportive environment that encourages student attendance and engagement.
34:15  Student Attendance and Enrollment Challenges
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Maintaining Neighborhood Schools: This is where our money is going. Some small schools have 200 students — this is not fiscally feasible, and not efficient. We would have to close half our schools and lay off half our staff to achieve efficiency.
- APS is facing the difficult decision of potentially closing and consolidating schools to better allocate resources. But this is not politically feasible. Other urban districts are doing this.
Babak Mostaghimi (Learner Studio, formerly Gwinnett County Public Schools)
- Chronic Absenteeism: Vivek highlighted that Gwinnett County is experiencing chronic absenteeism across all demographic groups. Issue is widespread and not confined to any particular segment of the student population.
- Rethinking Value Proposition: Parents are rethinking the value proposition of school, especially in light of the pandemic. Some parents may feel that taking a few days off for trips or other activities might be more beneficial than attending school, which poses a challenge for maintaining consistent attendance.
- Demographic Shift: As we have an influx of new students, they tend to be lower-income, non-English speakers. We have wealthier, white/Asian families move out. So even as our enrollment is going up, so are their needs.
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Data Analysis: Fayette County is analyzing data to understand where student movement is happening. Focus on identifying the grades and areas where students are leaving the district. Fifth graders are not going to 6th.
- Branding and Campaigning: The district is engaging in branding and campaigning efforts to retain students. For example, they are targeting parents of 4th and 5th graders to ensure they continue into 6th grade within the district.
- Safety Focus: Fayette County has centered its efforts on promoting the safety of their schools. The campaign has proven effective, as the district saw a 1% increase in attendance by emphasizing that schools are safe places for students.
39:15  Special Education Funding and Challenges
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Underfunding: Special education is grossly underfunded. The funding provided by federal sources covers only a small portion of the actual needs. Districts are supplementing it more than you can imagine — this is the biggest funding pain point.
- Staffing Costs: Most of the special education budget is allocated to staff, including school psychologists, nurses, speech therapists, and occupational therapists.
- Increasing Needs: There is a rising number of students with severe medical needs, such as those requiring feeding tubes and ventilators, which necessitates full-time nursing and specialized support. The growing number of students with significant medical and educational needs, who are thankfully getting better and living longer, also adds complexity and cost to providing appropriate support.
- Autism Classes: APS has seen a dramatic increase in the need for self-contained autism classes. The district opened eight new autism classes last year and plans to open more this year.
- We are up to 1 in 40 students in a low-incidence classrooms; we don’t know why, starting to study it. When I talk to my peers in other districts, the rate is increasing elsewhere as well
Priti Juhari (Milpitas Unified)
- Increasing Numbers: The number of students with special needs is increasing, particularly in categories like autism.
- Inclusion Classrooms: Milpitas Unified is expanding inclusion classrooms, starting from transitional kindergarten (TK) and moving up through the grades.
- Pandemic Impact: Recent research indicates that children who were babies during the pandemic may have developmental delays in fine motor skills and stamina, leading to potential misidentification of special needs.
- Misidentification: There is a challenge in distinguishing between actual special education needs and skills that have not yet been developed due to pandemic-related disruptions.
43:30  Advantages of Working with Startups vs. Larger Established Vendors
Ty Gorman (Barrington 220)
- Responsiveness: Startups tend to be very responsive and willing to co-develop solutions tailored to the district's specific needs. Over time, some startups have essentially created custom products for the district, which has been highly beneficial.
- Symbiotic Relationship: Startups benefit from trying their products in the district, while the district benefits from having a company that is responsive to their needs and willing to make changes based on feedback.
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Partnership Development: He emphasized the importance of building partnerships with startups, which allows for a more collaborative approach to developing and implementing solutions.
- Long-term Relationships: Houston values the ability to co-build solutions with startups, which creates a sense of ownership and investment in the product.
Babak Mostaghimi (Learner Studio, formerly Gwinnett County Public Schools)
- Challenges: Big districts face high friction costs when working with startups. The frequent changes and instability associated with startups can be problematic.
- Preference for Established Vendors: For large districts like Gwinnett, established vendors provide more stability and are less likely to introduce disruptive changes.
- Niche Innovation Work: While Gwinett does engage with startups, it is usually for niche innovation projects rather than core services.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Evidence-based Practices: Prefers vendors who can provide independent research and evidence to support the efficacy of their products.
- Outcomes-based Contracts: More appealing to Chelsea; shows that they are confident in their product’s effectiveness and are willing to put skin in the game.
- Caution with Rapid Changes: Teachers do not like rapid changes, which can be a downside of working with startups that frequently update or pivot their products.
48:50  Mental Health and Wellbeing for Students and Teachers
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Employee Wellness Division: Fayette County has established an Employee Wellness Division that includes counselors and therapists available to staff. These services are located in non-threatening, separate locations to encourage staff to seek help voluntarily.
- Additional Benefits: The district has increased benefits for employees, including providing disability insurance and faster access to mental health services. This comprehensive approach aims to support the overall well-being of staff.
- DESSA Screener: For students, the district uses the DESSA (Devereux Student Strengths Assessment) screener to identify mental health needs. Based on the results, the district recommends appropriate support services and interventions, which may include changes to schedules or additional resources.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Tiered Approach: APS employs a tiered approach to Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) and mental health.
- Technology Solutions: The district has experimented with technology solutions for therapy and mental health support. While some of these solutions have been successful, others have faced challenges, particularly around parent consent and the stigma associated with mental health services.
- Integration with Medicaid: Chelsea noted the complexity of integrating school-based mental health services with Medicaid. There is a need for better coordination to ensure that students who are eligible for Medicaid can access mental health services without the district incurring additional costs.
- Holistic Support: APS is focused on addressing non-academic needs, such as housing and food insecurity, to improve overall student well-being. The district is opening a centralized support hub to help families navigate these challenges, which in turn supports student attendance and engagement.
52:30  Navigating RFPs
Babak Mostaghimi (Learner Studio, formerly Gwinnett County Public Schools)
- Influencing RFPs Early: Vendors can sometimes impact RFPs by publishing white papers and sharing information that highlights new approaches or technologies. This can lead districts to incorporate these insights into their RFPs, making them more aligned with what vendors can offer.
Houston Barber (Fayette County Public Schools)
- Relationships Matter: Having established relationships — what’s working, what’s not, ways to work together — can help vendors understand district needs better and tailor their proposals accordingly. RFPs are important to ensure a fair and equitable process, and vendors should focus on understanding the specific strategies and actions of the district.
Chelsea Montgomery (Atlanta Public Schools)
- Evidence-based Practices: Chelsea stressed the importance of providing evidence-based practices in RFP responses. Vendors should include independent research and evidence to support the efficacy of their products.
- Understanding Best-fit Product: When crafting RFPs, Chelsea uses the [Hexagon framework from the National Implementation Research Network](https://implementation.fpg.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/NIRN-Hexagon-Discussion-Analysis-Tool_September2020_1.pdf) to ensure that the focus is not just on selecting the best product but also on successful implementation. Vendors should demonstrate how their solutions can be effectively implemented in the district.
