K-12 Funding Landscape
- Over $190 billion in federal ESSER funds have been allocated through three stimulus bills (ESSER I, II, III).
- Key obligation deadlines for these funds are September 30, 2022 (ESSER I), September 30, 2023 (ESSER II), and September 30, 2024 (ESSER III).
- The initial focus shifted from exigent COVID-19 response (e.g., Wi-Fi, PPE) to addressing mental health and "accelerating learning" beyond just "learning loss."
- These federal funds are largely supplemental, sitting atop generally healthy state and local revenue forecasts for schools.
Deploying Federal Funds
- There's a significant lag in fund deployment due to the time needed for strategic, multi-year planning and district operational challenges (e.g., staff covering classrooms).
- Funds are primarily on a reimbursement basis, meaning obligation precedes actual liquidation, offering flexibility to adjust plans.
- Large districts, such as Charlotte-Mecklenburg, purposefully keep around 30% of funds unobligated to maintain flexibility against future uncertainties.
- The K-12 sector's traditional slow spending patterns suggest a likelihood of federal deadline extensions.
Evolving District Priorities
- Department of Education guidance now emphasizes broad categories and "storytelling" over strict allowable expenditure lists, encouraging creative, interconnected uses of funds.
- Top district priorities include addressing lost instructional time, social-emotional and mental health, EdTech, facilities, and critically, teacher capacity and staffing.
- The definition of "educational success" is broadening beyond academic proficiency to encompass cognitive, non-cognitive, academic, and non-academic components.
- Tutoring is a high priority, but districts seek nuanced solutions that integrate with family engagement, staff recruitment, and high-quality instructional materials.
Navigating the 2024 Funding "Wave"
- The notion of a "funding cliff" in 2024 is misleading; stable state and local revenues present a "wave" of opportunity, unlike the 2010 ARRA situation.
- Districts are strategically "banking" general revenues, displacing pre-pandemic expenditures with federal funds to extend their investment window.
- This extended window allows for piloting innovative or effective programs, whether behavioral, academic, or aligned with new student success profiles.
- Funds are "fungible"; sophisticated larger districts can swap local/state dollars for federal funds through strategic journaling to maximize flexibility.
Shifting Marketplace Dynamics
- The assessment landscape is rapidly changing, with a decline in summative testing, a rise in formative, and state experiments like Virginia's "through-course" model.
- Student and staff well-being is now critical for school climate; mental health is increasingly viewed as a "gating issue" for effective learning.
- Increased public and political pressure demands financial transparency and evidence-based programming, requiring strong vendor partnerships to justify expenditures.
- Parent engagement has become a crucial constituency, influencing curriculum visibility and driving district enrollment concerns (e.g., states considering bills to post curriculum online).
Key Investment Focus Areas
- Early Learners: Increased acceptance of technology, strong focus on early literacy, addressing high absenteeism rates, and leveraging parental engagement for enrollment.
- Special Education: Acute staffing shortages, districts grappling with backlogs in legal liabilities for related services, and elevated responsibilities due to "appropriate" education standards (post-2017 *Endrew F.* Supreme Court decision).
- Teacher Capacity: A significant challenge hindering new in-class behavioral/SEL practices, leading districts to use federal funds for more contracted support and consultants.
- Attendance: A very high priority, seen as a proxy for student engagement and directly impacting district revenue, driving a need for better parent/student touchpoints.
