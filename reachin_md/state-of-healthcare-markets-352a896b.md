How Health Tech Has Performed in Public Markets
- Health tech has underperformed the IGV and S&P 500 since ChatGPT launched in late 2022, as investor attention shifted to AI infrastructure. Nvidia is up roughly 950% over that same period.
- Health tech has outperformed since the Trump election win. The current administration has been more favorable to technology in drug development, clinical trials, and care delivery. HHS in particular has been pushing programs to integrate technology and drive better outcomes.
- The defining shift: health tech 1.0 (Teladoc, Livongo) was built on engagement. Health tech 2.0 is built on outcomes -- clinical results and financial ROI. Companies delivering both are winning.
Recent IPOs and What the Market Rewards
- Outperformers: Hinge Health and Tempus. Both demonstrate strong clinical and financial outcomes.Underperformer: Omada. Beats expectations every quarter, but ~60% of revenue ties to Cigna and investors are skeptical as GLP-1 tailwinds fade.Two valuation buckets: Vertical SaaS (70%+ gross margins, multi-year contracts) trades at 4-6x revenue. Tech-enabled services (human-led, sub-70% margins) trades closer to 2x. Hinge sits in the first bucket; Omada in the second.
- Health tech got swept into the "SaaS-pocalypse" as investors questioned whether general AI models could displace these businesses.
AI Disruption and Business Model Durability
- Acuity determines automation. Low-acuity care (MSK, routine primary care) can be largely automated. Higher-acuity care (depression, specialty medicine) will always need a human in the loop.
- General LLMs are avoiding healthcare. No appetite for HIPAA, PHI, or de-identified data. This gives domain-specific companies a window to build moats.
- Scribe market is maturing fast. Epic, Cerner, and Athena are building scribe natively. Vendors that can't move into RCM before EHRs absorb the use case will lose ground.
- RCM is the clearest near-term AI ROI. Health systems average 15-20% claims denial rates. AI is bringing that down with real savings on both revenue and labor.
M&A Trends
- 2025 saw a strong recovery in deal activity versus 2024, aided by a more deal-friendly administration.
- Q1 2026 deal count is down year over year. Choppiness in private credit is making PE buyers cautious on larger transactions.
- Most take-privates are targeting health tech 1.0 companies for re-platforming or asset combination.
What Investors Look at for Private Company Valuation
Top three metrics across both software and tech-enabled services:
- Revenue growth rate
- Gross dollar retention
- Rule of 40 (growth rate + free cash flow margin)
Additional signals for software: gross margin (70% is the SaaS line), customer concentration, percent recurring revenue, net dollar retention, payback period.
Where Investment Is Flowing
- RCM and clinical documentation: ~40% of clinician time goes to non-patient tasks. Health systems are prioritizing automation.
- Value-based care---including ACOs, home-based care, and care gap closure---is expanding into cardiology, oncology, nephrology.
- Life sciences: Shifting from drug discovery AI to R&D acceleration and clinical trial efficiency.
- Direct-to-consumer and proactive health: GLP-1, longevity, wearables, function testing. ~50% of millennials and Gen Z have no PCP. Companies to watch: Oura, Whoop, Fava, Wellhub, Midi Health, Function Health.
- Pediatric mental health. ChatGPT sees 342M therapy and companionship conversations per week. None of the major LLMs escalate mental health risk to a human appropriately (per Spring Health's Vera MH model).
- Femtech: predictions that fertility care expands into early parenting, then into pediatric and family health as a natural LTV extension.
